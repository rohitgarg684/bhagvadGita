1. Shloka
अभ्यासेऽप्यसमर्थोऽसि मत्कर्मपरमो भव ।
मदर्थमपि कर्माणि कुर्वन् सिद्धिमवाप्स्यसि ॥12.10॥
abhyāse'pyasamartho'si matkarmaparamo bhava ।
madarthamapi karmāṇi kurvan siddhimavāpsyasi ॥12.10॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says: if we are unable even to practice abhyāsa-yoga, then we should become dedicated to actions done for Bhagavān, and through such action we will attain perfection.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now comes down to the next step in the ladder of bhakti-yoga. If a person is not able to practice abhyāsa-yoga — the repeated meditation upon an iṣṭa-devatā or sacred symbol — then Kṛṣṇa gives another accessible discipline: become matkarmaparamaḥ. This means making Bhagavān-related action the priority.
Here matkarma means action offered to Bhagavān. It is īśvara-arpaṇa karma, niṣkāma karma, action that contributes to citta-śuddhi. Such action is not merely activity. It is action done as service, contribution, and paropakāra, with the attitude that this is worship of Viśvarūpa īśvara.
The verse says madarthamapi karmāṇi kurvan — even by performing actions for My sake. This means we may begin with practical, outward action, but the inner attitude changes. We act not merely for personal gain, recognition, or comfort, but as an offering to Bhagavān.
Kṛṣṇa then says siddhimavāpsyasi — you will attain perfection. This does not mean that action by itself directly gives mokṣa. The progression is important: niṣkāma karma purifies the mind; that mind becomes ready for eka-rūpa upāsanā; then aneka-rūpa or Viśvarūpa upāsanā; then aham brahmāsmi jñānam; and through that knowledge comes freedom.
So the verse is deeply practical. If meditation is not yet possible, we should not give up spiritual life. We begin with selfless action. We serve, contribute, reduce selfishness, and transform daily work into a means of inner purification.
4. Story
In the Mahābhārata, the story of King Śibi beautifully illustrates the spirit of action done for dharma and the welfare of another.
One day, a frightened dove flew into King Śibi’s court and fell into his lap. Its body trembled, and its eyes were filled with fear. Behind it came a fierce hawk, demanding the dove as its rightful food.
The king faced a difficult situation. The dove had sought refuge, and a king must protect one who surrenders. But the hawk too had a claim — it needed food to live.
Śibi did not dismiss either side. He thought carefully and then made a remarkable offer. He said to the hawk, “Do not take this dove. I will give you flesh from my own body equal to its weight.”
A balance was brought. The dove was placed on one side, and Śibi cut flesh from his own body and placed it on the other. But no matter how much he gave, the dove still weighed more.
At last, Śibi himself climbed onto the scale.
He was ready to offer his whole body to protect one who had come seeking refuge.
This was not ordinary charity. It was action done without selfish calculation. The king did not ask, “What will I gain?” He saw duty, compassion, and protection as sacred. His action became worship through sacrifice.
This is the spirit of matkarma and madartham karma. When action is freed from narrow self-interest and done as service to dharma and Bhagavān’s order, it purifies the heart.
5. Impact to Current Life
Many of us struggle with meditation. Sitting quietly, visualizing Bhagavān, or maintaining inward attention may feel difficult. The mind may be too restless, extroverted, or filled with responsibilities. This verse gives hope: we can still begin through action.
Everyday life gives countless opportunities for matkarma. Helping at home, doing honest work, serving society, teaching, supporting someone in distress, giving time, sharing knowledge, or performing duties without selfishness can all become part of spiritual growth.
The important shift is the attitude. If we work only for praise, money, control, or personal comfort, the mind remains narrow. But when action becomes offering, the mind begins to expand.
This is especially relevant today. We often measure success by how much we get. This verse invites us to measure spiritual growth by how much we give, serve, contribute, and purify the mind through action.
6. Reflection
Can I transform some part of my daily work into an offering to Bhagavān?
Do my actions increase selfishness, or do they help purify and expand my mind?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Sometimes sitting quietly and meditating is hard. Your mind may run around.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says, ‘That is okay. Start by doing good actions for Me.’
So when you help someone, clean your room, study sincerely, or share something kindly, you can think, ‘I am doing this as an offering to Bhagavān.’
Then even ordinary work becomes special.”
7B. Story
A child found it hard to sit quietly for prayer.
Every time the child closed the eyes, thoughts started running everywhere.
So the parent said, “That is okay. Today, help me serve food to everyone. Do it carefully and happily, and think, ‘I am offering this to Bhagavān.’”
The child helped serve food, filled water glasses, and smiled at everyone.
At the end, the child felt peaceful.
The parent said, “See? You worshipped through action.”
The child understood that prayer is not only sitting silently. Helping with love can also become worship.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What is one helpful action you can do today as an offering?
Can you help someone without asking, “What will I get?”
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: abhyāse'pi asamarthaḥ asi = if you are unable even for abhyāsa
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is addressing one who is not yet able to practice abhyāsa-yoga, the repeated meditation on an iṣṭa-devatā or sacred symbol. Therefore, He gives a still more accessible step.
Step 2: The New Discipline
Phrase: matkarmaparamaḥ bhava = become one for whom My work is supreme
We should take up actions connected with Bhagavān. Matkarma means īśvara-arpaṇa karma — niṣkāma karma, actions done in a spirit of offering. These actions purify the mind and prepare it for the next step.
Step 3: Nature of the Action
Phrase: madarthamapi karmāṇi = actions even for My sake
The actions are done for Bhagavān’s sake, not merely for personal gain. This includes service, contribution, paropakāra, and duties performed as worship of Viśvarūpa īśvara.
Step 4: Active Participation
Phrase: kurvan = performing
This path does not ask us to withdraw from activity. We remain active, but the attitude changes. The same life becomes spiritualized when action is offered to Bhagavān.
Step 5: The Result Promised
Phrase: siddhimavāpsyasi = you will attain perfection
Kṛṣṇa promises that such a life leads to siddhi. This siddhi is mokṣa, but the connection is through a sequence. Niṣkāma karma prepares the mind for eka-rūpa upāsanā, then Viśvarūpa upāsanā, then aham brahmāsmi jñānam, and finally liberation.
Step 6: The Teaching
Phrase: matkarmaparamo bhava … siddhimavāpsyasi = become dedicated to My work, and you will attain perfection
The teaching is that no one is excluded from spiritual growth. If meditation is difficult, begin with selfless action. Action done as offering becomes the doorway to purity, maturity, and eventual freedom.

9E. Sandhi
अभ्यासे + अपि = अभ्यासेऽपि
Sandhi name: पूर्वरूपसन्धिः; final ए remains, and the following अ is elided, indicated by अवग्रहः.
अपि + असमर्थः = अप्यसमर्थः
Sandhi name: यण् सन्धिः; final इ becomes य् before the following vowel अ.
असमर्थः + असि = असमर्थोऽसि
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final अः becomes ओ before अ, with avagraha marking the elided अ.
मत्कर्मपरमः + भव = मत्कर्मपरमो भव
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final अः becomes ओ before the soft consonant भ.
मदर्थम् + अपि = मदर्थमपि
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel अ without special change.
सिद्धिम् + अवाप्स्यसि = सिद्धिमवाप्स्यसि
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel अ without special change.
9F. Samāsa
मत्कर्मपरमः = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: मत्कर्म परमं यस्य सः — one for whom My work is supreme.
मदर्थम् = तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: मम अर्थे — for My sake.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
कुर्वन् is a शतृप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from कृ धातुः, meaning “performing.”
भव is लोट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम्, from भू धातुः, used as an instruction.
असि is लट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम्, from अस् धातुः.
अवाप्स्यसि is लृट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम्, from अव-आप् धातुः, indicating future attainment.
मदर्थम् is used adverbially in the sense “for My sake.”
सिद्धिम् is द्वितीया-विभक्ति and is the object of अवाप्स्यसि.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Maharṣi Dadhīci (Purāṇic Tradition)
The devas once faced a terrible danger. Vṛtra, a powerful asura, had become almost impossible to defeat. The worlds were disturbed, and the devas sought a way to restore order.
They learned that the weapon needed to defeat Vṛtra could be made only from the bones of the great sage Dadhīci.
This was not a small request. To give his bones meant to give up the body itself.
When the devas approached Dadhīci, he did not respond with fear or resentment. He understood that the body is temporary, and if it could be used for the welfare of the world, then even that could become an offering.
With calmness and clarity, Dadhīci gave up his body so that the vajra could be fashioned and dharma could be protected.
This story powerfully illustrates niṣkāma karma. Dadhīci did not act for personal gain. His very body became an offering for the welfare of all.
This is the spirit of madartham karma — action done not for the narrow self, but as service in Bhagavān’s order.
2. Story of Bharata Ruling for Rāma (Rāmāyaṇa)
When Bharata returned to Ayodhyā and learned that Rāma had been sent to the forest, he was devastated. The kingdom had come to him, but he did not see it as a personal prize.
He went to the forest and begged Rāma to return. But Rāma would not violate dharma or His father’s word.
Bharata then took Rāma’s pādukās and placed them on the throne.
He ruled Ayodhyā not as an owner, but as a caretaker. Every decision, every responsibility, every act of governance was done in Rāma’s name.
He lived simply, with discipline, waiting for Rāma’s return.
This is a beautiful example of action done as offering. Bharata did not abandon duty. He performed duty without possessiveness, keeping Bhagavān Rāma as the true center.
His kingship became matkarma — work done for Bhagavān.
3. Story of Eknāth Mahārāj and the Thirsty Donkey
Sant Eknāth once carried sacred Gaṅgā water with great care. It was meant for worship, and those around him treated it as precious.
On the way, he saw a donkey suffering terribly from thirst.
Others might have said, “This water is only for ritual use.” But Eknāth saw the suffering being before him and recognized an opportunity for real worship.
He gave the sacred water to the thirsty donkey.
Some people criticized him, thinking he had wasted what was meant for a holy purpose.
But Eknāth’s understanding was deeper. Service to a suffering being was not outside worship. Compassion itself became worship.
This story shows how action becomes sacred when done without selfishness. Service, when offered to Bhagavān present in all beings, becomes īśvara-arpaṇa karma.
4. Story of Swāmī Vivekānanda and Daridra Nārāyaṇa Sevā
Swāmī Vivekānanda traveled across India and saw great suffering — poverty, hunger, ignorance, and helplessness. His heart was deeply moved.
He did not see spirituality as something confined to meditation rooms or temples alone. He taught that serving human beings is serving Nārāyaṇa Himself, especially when we serve the poor, weak, and suffering.
This gave rise to the ideal of Daridra Nārāyaṇa sevā — seeing Bhagavān in those who need help.
For him, service was not mere social work. It was worship. Feeding, teaching, healing, and uplifting others could become spiritual discipline when done with the right vision.
This directly reflects the teaching of 12.10. If meditation is difficult, begin with service. Let action purify the heart. Let contribution become worship.
5. Story of Bhagīratha Bringing Gaṅgā
King Bhagīratha undertook an immense effort to bring Gaṅgā down to earth for the welfare of his ancestors.
This was not a small task. He performed austerities with great dedication. He prayed to Gaṅgā. He prayed to Bhagavān Śiva, because only Śiva could receive the force of Gaṅgā’s descent.
Again and again, Bhagīratha continued without giving up.
His effort was not for personal pleasure or fame. It was for a sacred purpose — to uplift others and fulfill dharma.
When Gaṅgā finally descended, it was not only his ancestors who benefited. The whole world received the blessing of her waters.
Bhagīratha’s story shows how sustained action done for a sacred purpose becomes purifying. When work is dedicated to a higher cause, it becomes a path of spiritual growth.
