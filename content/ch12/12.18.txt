1. Shloka
समः शत्रौ च मित्रे च तथा मानापमानयोः ।
शीतोष्णसुखदुःखेषु समः सङ्गविवर्जितः ॥12.18॥
samaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca tathā mānāpamānayoḥ ।
śītoṣṇasukhaduḥkheṣu samaḥ saṅgavivarjitaḥ ॥12.18॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the mature devotee is the same toward enemy and friend, honor and dishonor, heat and cold, pleasure and pain, and is free from attachment.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa continues describing the parā-bhakta, the mature devotee who has gone through the disciplines of bhakti-yoga and has gained inner steadiness. In this verse, the central word is samaḥ — the one who remains the same.
First, the devotee is samaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca — the same toward enemy and friend. This does not mean a friend and an enemy are treated identically in practical life. A friend may be welcomed; an enemy may require caution. But inwardly, the devotee is not ruled by attachment toward the friend or hatred toward the enemy. The response is governed by dharma and clarity, not by emotional partiality.
Then Bhagavān says the devotee is the same in māna and apamāna — honor and dishonor. Praise, respect, recognition, insult, neglect, and humiliation do not become the basis of the person’s self-worth. The mature devotee may receive honor without inflation and dishonor without inner collapse.
The same steadiness applies to śīta-uṣṇa and sukha-duḥkha — heat and cold, pleasure and pain. Physical and emotional experiences continue to come and go, but the devotee is not internally enslaved by them.
The verse ends with saṅga-vivarjitaḥ — free from attachment. This is the foundation of the previous qualities. Attachment creates dependence; dependence creates fear, partiality, and agitation. When attachment is loosened, the mind becomes available for sameness.
4. Story
The life of Haridāsa Ṭhākura gives a powerful example of this verse.
Haridāsa Ṭhākura was deeply devoted to nāma-saṅkīrtana, the repetition and singing of Bhagavān’s name. His life was centered on remembrance of Bhagavān, not on social approval.
But not everyone honored him. Some people opposed him because of religious and social prejudice. His devotion was misunderstood, and he was punished severely.
In one famous incident, he was ordered to be beaten in public marketplaces.
The punishment was brutal. The people who struck him were not friends. They were acting as enemies. The body suffered. The situation was humiliating. Onlookers watched. The world could easily have become divided in his mind: “These are my enemies; these are my supporters; this is honor; this is dishonor; this is pain; this is injustice.”
But Haridāsa Ṭhākura’s mind did not turn to hatred.
As he was beaten, he continued remembering Bhagavān. More than that, he prayed for those who were hurting him. He did not want them to suffer the consequences of their action.
This is not ordinary tolerance. It is deep inner freedom.
Pain was there, but hatred did not arise. Dishonor was there, but his dignity did not depend on public respect. Enemies were there, but his heart did not become poisoned.
This story beautifully reflects samaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca, mānāpamānayoḥ samaḥ, and sukha-duḥkheṣu samaḥ. The mature devotee is not made or broken by how the world behaves.
5. Impact to Current Life
Modern life constantly divides people into “my side” and “their side.” We do this in families, workplaces, politics, religion, online discussions, and even spiritual communities. Once someone becomes “enemy” in our mind, we easily justify hatred. Once someone becomes “my person,” we may overlook wrong behavior.
This verse asks for maturity. We can recognize difference in behavior without hatred in the heart. We can love a friend without blind attachment. We can respond to an opponent without inner poison.
Honor and dishonor are also powerful today. A small praise can make us feel inflated; a small criticism can disturb us for days. Social media has made this worse. Likes, comments, recognition, and public image easily become emotional food.
The teaching is not to become dull or indifferent. It is to stop outsourcing our inner balance to the world. Weather will change, people will change, comfort will come and go, praise and blame will alternate. The mature mind remains anchored.
6. Reflection
Do I treat people according to dharma, or according to attachment and dislike?
How much does my peace depend on praise, comfort, recognition, and approval?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Sometimes someone is nice to us, and we call them our friend. Sometimes someone is not nice, and we may feel they are against us.
Kṛṣṇa is saying that a good devotee should not become full of hatred toward one person or too attached to another. We should try to be fair and kind.
Also, if someone praises us, we should not become too proud. If someone criticizes us, we should not feel completely broken. We should stay steady inside.”
7B. Story
A child brought a drawing to school.
One friend said, “This is beautiful!”
The child felt very happy.
Then another child said, “I don’t like it.”
The child suddenly felt sad and wanted to tear the drawing.
The teacher gently said, “Pause. One person praised it. One person did not. But the drawing is still your work. Can you listen calmly and keep learning?”
The child looked again at the drawing.
The child smiled and said, “I can improve it, but I do not have to feel bad.”
That day, the child learned not to become too proud from praise or too sad from criticism.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What happens to your mind when someone praises you?
Can you stay calm when someone says something you do not like?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Sameness Toward Enemy and Friend
Phrase: samaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca = the same toward enemy and friend
The mature devotee is not ruled by hatred toward an enemy or attachment toward a friend. Practical responses may differ, but the inner mind remains balanced.
Step 2: Sameness in Honor and Dishonor
Phrase: tathā mānāpamānayoḥ = likewise in honor and dishonor
The devotee is not inflated by honor and not shattered by dishonor. Respect and insult are received as passing experiences, not as definitions of the Self.
Step 3: Sameness in Heat and Cold
Phrase: śītoṣṇa = cold and heat
Physical conditions such as heat and cold continue to affect the body. But the mature mind does not allow bodily discomfort to dominate inner clarity.
Step 4: Sameness in Pleasure and Pain
Phrase: sukha-duḥkheṣu samaḥ = the same in pleasure and pain
Pleasure and pain arise in life, but the parā-bhakta remains steady. This does not mean the absence of sensation or feeling; it means freedom from inner slavery to them.
Step 5: Freedom from Attachment
Phrase: saṅga-vivarjitaḥ = free from attachment
Attachment is the root that makes the mind partial and disturbed. When attachment is loosened, the mind can remain balanced in friend and enemy, honor and dishonor, comfort and discomfort.
Step 6: The Teaching
Phrase: samaḥ … samaḥ saṅga-vivarjitaḥ = the same, the same, free from attachment
The repeated emphasis on sameness shows that the parā-bhakta’s stability is not situational. It is born of inner freedom. The world of pairs continues, but the devotee is not tossed about by the pairs.

9E. Sandhi
मान + अपमानयोः = मानापमानयोः
Sandhi name: सवर्णदीर्घसन्धिः; final अ and initial अ combine into आ.
शीत + उष्ण = शीतोष्ण
Sandhi name: गुणसन्धिः; अ + उ becomes ओ.
सुख + दुःखेषु = सुखदुःखेषु
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final अ joins with द् without special phonetic change.
सङ्ग + विवर्जितः = सङ्गविवर्जितः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final अ joins with व् without special phonetic change.
9F. Samāsa
मानापमानयोः = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: मानः च अपमानः च — honor and dishonor.
शीतोष्ण = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: शीतम् च उष्णम् च — cold and heat.
सुखदुःखेषु = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: सुखम् च दुःखम् च — pleasure and pain.
शीतोष्णसुखदुःखेषु = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: शीतम् च उष्णम् च सुखम् च दुःखम् च — in cold, heat, pleasure, and pain.
सङ्गविवर्जितः = तृतीया तत्पुरुष / बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: सङ्गेन विवर्जितः — one who is free from attachment.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
विवर्जितः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from वर्ज् धातुः with वि-उपसर्गः, meaning “devoid of” or “free from.”
शत्रौ and मित्रे are सप्तमी-एकवचनम् forms, indicating the field in which sameness is maintained.
मानापमानयोः is सप्तमी-द्विवचनम् because the pair honor-dishonor is treated as two.
शीतोष्णसुखदुःखेषु is सप्तमी-बहुवचनम् because multiple pairs are included.
समः appears twice for emphasis, showing sameness in both relational and experiential situations.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Guru Nānak, Bhāī Lālo, and Malik Bhāgo
Guru Nānak once came to a town where two very different invitations awaited him.
Bhāī Lālo was a poor carpenter. His home was simple, and the food he could offer was plain. But it was earned honestly and offered with devotion.
Malik Bhāgo was wealthy and powerful. He arranged a grand feast and expected Guru Nānak to attend. There would be honor, display, and social prestige.
Guru Nānak chose to stay with Bhāī Lālo.
Malik Bhāgo felt insulted and questioned him. Why would a saint reject a rich man’s feast and accept the food of a poor carpenter?
Guru Nānak did not answer with anger. He showed the truth through an unforgettable act. Taking food from Bhāī Lālo and food from Malik Bhāgo, he pressed them. From Bhāī Lālo’s food came the sweetness of honest labor; from Malik Bhāgo’s food came the suffering caused by exploitation.
The point was not poverty versus wealth. The point was purity versus impurity.
Guru Nānak was not attracted by honor, nor afraid of displeasing power. He was not attached to comfort or status. He responded according to dharma.
This reflects mānāpamānayoḥ samaḥ and saṅga-vivarjitaḥ.
2. Story of Narsiṁha Mehtā and Social Humiliation
Narsiṁha Mehtā was a devotee of Kṛṣṇa whose heart overflowed in song and devotion. But society around him did not always honor him.
He moved freely among devotees, sang with love, and cared more for Bhagavān than for social approval. Because of this, some people mocked him and excluded him. They measured him by social standards; he measured life by devotion.
In one remembered incident, he was humiliated by people who doubted his worth and devotion. Instead of responding with anger or bitterness, he turned more deeply toward Kṛṣṇa.
His songs did not become songs of revenge. They became songs of surrender.
For Narsiṁha Mehtā, public honor was not the foundation of life. Dishonor too could not take away his inner wealth. His companionship was with Bhagavān.
This story reflects mānāpamānayoḥ samaḥ. The one who belongs inwardly to Bhagavān is not made big by honor or small by dishonor.
3. Story of Sītā in Aśoka-vana
In the Rāmāyaṇa, Sītā was held in Aśoka-vana in Laṅkā. She was surrounded by rākṣasīs, threatened by Rāvaṇa, and separated from Rāma.
The outer conditions were painful. There was no comfort, no royal honor, no familiar family, and no physical security. Rāvaṇa tried to tempt her with wealth, status, and queenly power. He also tried to frighten her.
But Sītā’s mind did not bend.
She did not hate in a way that made her lose dignity, and she did not crave the comforts being offered. She did not measure herself by the humiliation of captivity. Her heart remained steady in dharma and in devotion to Rāma.
Trijaṭā and others saw that Sītā’s strength was not outer strength. It was inner anchoring.
This reflects sukha-duḥkheṣu samaḥ and saṅga-vivarjitaḥ. Comfort and discomfort came; honor and dishonor came; threat and temptation came. But her inner commitment did not shift.
4. Story of Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār and the Temple Priest
Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār was a great devotee of Bhagavān Raṅganātha. Yet because of social prejudice, he was not allowed to enter the temple.
One day, while he was absorbed in devotion near the river, the temple priest Loka Sāranga came to collect water for worship. Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār, absorbed in Bhagavān, did not notice him immediately.
The priest became upset and threw a small stone to make him move.
Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār was hurt, but he did not respond with anger. He moved away quietly, feeling that he had obstructed service to Bhagavān.
Later, Bhagavān Raṅganātha appeared in the priest’s dream and revealed that the hurt given to the devotee had reached Bhagavān Himself. The priest was instructed to bring Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār into the temple on his shoulders.
The next day, the priest carried him into the sanctum. Tiruppāṇ Āḻvār beheld Bhagavān Raṅganātha and poured out the verses of the Amalan Ādhipirān.
This story shows sameness in dishonor and honor. When insulted, he did not hate. When honored and carried into the temple, he did not become proud. His mind remained only with Bhagavān.
5. Story of Lallā Ded Walking Through Praise and Blame
Lallā Ded of Kashmir lived as a mystic whose life did not fit ordinary social expectations.
Some people revered her as a saint. Others mocked her, misunderstood her, or judged her harshly. Praise and blame followed her as she moved through villages and fields.
But Lallā Ded did not build her identity on either.
She continued speaking from the depth of realization, using simple sayings that pointed people inward. Her life showed freedom from social approval and disapproval.
When people praised, she did not become inflated. When people blamed, she did not become broken. She had already turned away from dependence on outer labels.
This reflects mānāpamānayoḥ samaḥ and saṅga-vivarjitaḥ. The one who is free from attachment to image can move through praise and blame with inner steadiness.
