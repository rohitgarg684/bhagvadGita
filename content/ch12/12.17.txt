1. Shloka
यो न हृष्यति न द्वेष्टि न शोचति न काङ्क्षति ।
शुभाशुभपरित्यागी भक्तिमान्यः स मे प्रियः ॥12.17॥
yo na hṛṣyati na dveṣṭi na śocati na kāṅkṣati ।
śubhāśubhaparityāgī bhaktimānyaḥ sa me priyaḥ ॥12.17॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the devotee who is not overpowered by elation, hatred, grief, or craving, and who gives up both puṇya and pāpa as binding pursuits, is dear to Him.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa continues describing the parā-bhakta, the mature devotee who has gained inner strength through knowledge and spiritual maturity. This verse focuses on freedom from emotional extremes.
The words na hṛṣyati and na śocati should be understood together. The devotee is not overpowered by elation when favorable situations come, and is not overpowered by depression when unfavorable situations come. Happiness and disturbance may naturally arise, but they do not become so intense that they cloud viveka, the discriminative faculty.
Similarly, na dveṣṭi and na kāṅkṣati go together. The devotee is free from binding dveṣa and binding rāga. Dveṣa is strong aversion, the tendency to reject, hate, or resist what is unpleasant. Kāṅkṣā is craving, the mind’s insistence that something must come for me to be complete. The parā-bhakta may function in the world, but is not inwardly ruled by these two forces.
The phrase śubhāśubhaparityāgī means one who has given up both auspicious and inauspicious as binding pursuits. This does not mean such a person avoids noble action. Noble actions may still be done, but not for the sake of collecting puṇya or gaining a better future experience. Puṇya and pāpa both keep one connected to saṁsāra. Puṇya is like a smiling binding force; pāpa is like a frowning binding force. The mature devotee does not act for either. Noble actions are done for citta-śuddhi, jñāna-niṣṭhā, dharma, or loka-saṅgraha, not for personal karmic acquisition.
Such a devotee is bhaktimān — endowed with mature devotion. This devotion is not emotional dependence. It is the devotion of one whose mind has become steady, whose reactions do not overpower viveka, and whose actions are free from binding self-interest. Such a devotee is dear to Bhagavān.
4. Story
In the Rāmāyaṇa, when the news came that Rāma must go to the forest, Ayodhyā was shaken.
The coronation had been prepared. The city was ready to celebrate. Rāma was to be crowned, and everyone expected joy, honor, and prosperity.
Then everything changed.
Because of Kaikeyī’s boons, Daśaratha had to send Rāma to the forest for fourteen years. The news was unbearable to many, especially Lakṣmaṇa.
Lakṣmaṇa’s love for Rāma was intense. When he heard what had happened, anger rose like fire. He could not tolerate what he saw as injustice. He spoke harshly about Daśaratha and Kaikeyī. In the heat of emotion, his discrimination became clouded. He even spoke of fighting against the decision.
This is what happens when emotional upheaval becomes too intense. Love was there. Dharma was there. But anger began to cover viveka.
Rāma, however, stood differently.
He did not rejoice when coronation was announced, and he did not collapse when exile was declared. He did not hate Kaikeyī. He did not grieve in a way that destroyed his clarity. He did not crave the kingdom that was taken away.
He calmly accepted the situation as dharma.
Then he gently cooled Lakṣmaṇa’s mind. He helped him see that action must not come from rage. Even in pain, the intellect must remain clear.
This story beautifully illustrates na hṛṣyati, na dveṣṭi, na śocati, and na kāṅkṣati. Emotional reactions may arise, but the parā-bhakta does not allow them to overpower viveka.
5. Impact to Current Life
Modern life constantly pulls us into emotional extremes. A promotion comes, and we become over-excited. A criticism comes, and we feel crushed. Someone succeeds, and envy appears. A plan fails, and grief or anger takes over.
This verse does not say we should become emotionless. It says we should not become overpowered. There is a difference between feeling an emotion and being ruled by it.
The most practical teaching here is to preserve inner space. When anger, excitement, grief, or craving arises, we should be able to notice: “My mind is becoming disturbed. My discrimination may be getting clouded.” That much awareness allows us to postpone harmful speech and action.
This is especially important in family life, workplace conflicts, emails, messages, and social media. Many mistakes happen because we respond while emotionally clouded. If we pause, the same situation can be handled with greater clarity.
The verse also challenges our obsession with results. Even good actions can become binding if they are done only to collect puṇya, recognition, superiority, or future reward. The mature way is to do what is right because it is right, not because we want to emotionally possess the result.
6. Reflection
When strong emotion comes, do I still have enough inner space to protect my viveka?
Do I do good actions for inner maturity and dharma, or mainly to gain recognition, puṇya, or reward?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Sometimes good things happen and we become very excited. Sometimes bad things happen and we become very sad or angry.
Kṛṣṇa is not saying, ‘Do not feel anything.’ He is saying, ‘Do not let feelings take control of you.’
If you are very angry, wait before speaking. If you are very excited, still think clearly. A good devotee keeps the mind steady.”
7B. Story
A child was playing a board game.
At first, the child was winning and became very excited. The child laughed loudly and teased the others.
Then suddenly the game changed, and the child started losing.
Now the child became angry and wanted to throw the pieces.
The parent said, “Pause. Winning and losing are both part of the game. Can you enjoy the game without becoming too excited or too upset?”
The child took a deep breath.
The game continued. This time, the child played more calmly.
Kṛṣṇa is teaching us the same thing: enjoy what comes, face what comes, but do not let the mind lose balance.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What do you do when you feel very angry or very excited?
Can you pause before speaking when your feelings are very strong?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Freedom from Overpowering Elation
Phrase: yo na hṛṣyati = one who does not become over-elated
The devotee is not carried away by extreme excitement when favorable situations come. Happiness may arise, but it does not become so intense that it clouds discrimination.
Step 2: Freedom from Hatred
Phrase: na dveṣṭi = does not hate / does not fall into aversion
The devotee is free from binding dveṣa. Unpleasant situations may come, but the mind does not convert them into hatred, rejection, or revengeful resistance.
Step 3: Freedom from Overpowering Grief
Phrase: na śocati = does not grieve
This does not mean there is no human sadness. It means the devotee is not overpowered by depression or sorrow. Emotional pain does not destroy viveka.
Step 4: Freedom from Craving
Phrase: na kāṅkṣati = does not crave / does not long for acquisition
The devotee is not ruled by rāga. The mind does not insist that a particular object, person, outcome, or situation is necessary for completion.
Step 5: The Pairs to Be Read Together
Phrase: na hṛṣyati … na śocati and na dveṣṭi … na kāṅkṣati = neither elation nor depression; neither aversion nor craving
The first pair refers to emotional reactions to favorable and unfavorable situations. The second pair refers to the underlying forces of rāga and dveṣa. The teaching is not emotional suppression, but emotional mastery.
Step 6: Renunciation of Good and Evil as Binding Pursuits
Phrase: śubhāśubhaparityāgī = one who has renounced both auspicious and inauspicious
The devotee gives up both pāpa and puṇya as binding pursuits. Pāpa obviously binds, but puṇya also binds when pursued for personal karmic enjoyment. Noble actions may still be performed, but not for egoistic acquisition of puṇya.
Step 7: Mature Devotion
Phrase: bhaktimānyaḥ sa me priyaḥ = such a devoted one is dear to Me
This is not ordinary emotional devotion. This is mature devotion in which the mind is steady, viveka is protected, rāga-dveṣa are weakened, and action is free from binding pursuit of karmic results.

9E. Sandhi
शुभ + अशुभ = शुभाशुभ
Sandhi name: सवर्णदीर्घसन्धिः; अ + अ becomes आ.
भक्तिमान् + यः = भक्तिमान्यः
Sandhi name: संयोगसन्धिः; final न् joins with य् to form न्य in continuous pronunciation.
सः + मे = स मे
Sandhi name: विसर्गलोपः; final visarga is dropped before the following consonant म्.
9F. Samāsa
शुभाशुभ = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: शुभम् च अशुभम् च — auspicious and inauspicious.
शुभाशुभपरित्यागी = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष / बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: शुभस्य अशुभस्य च परित्यागः यस्य सः — one who has renounced both auspicious and inauspicious as binding pursuits.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
हृष्यति, द्वेष्टि, शोचति, and काङ्क्षति are all लट्-लकारः, प्रथमपुरुषः, एकवचनम् forms.
भक्तिमान् is formed with मतुप्-प्रत्ययः, meaning “one who possesses bhakti.”
परित्यागी has the त्यागिन् sense, meaning “one who renounces.”
The repeated न emphasizes freedom from the four reactions: elation, hatred, grief, and craving.
The first यः and the later यः both refer to the same implied devotee; सः मे प्रियः completes the sentence.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Yudhiṣṭhira at the Yakṣa Lake
During the forest exile, the Pāṇḍavas once became terribly thirsty. One by one, the brothers went in search of water and reached a beautiful lake.
But a mysterious voice warned them: “Do not drink before answering my questions.”
Driven by thirst, the brothers ignored the warning and fell lifeless near the lake.
Finally Yudhiṣṭhira arrived.
He saw his brothers lying still. The sight was unbearable. These were not strangers; they were his own brothers, his strength, his companions in exile. Grief could have overwhelmed him. Anger could have risen. Desperation could have made him rush to drink.
But Yudhiṣṭhira paused.
He heard the Yakṣa’s warning and did not allow sorrow to cloud his judgment. He answered question after question with clarity. His viveka remained available even in crisis.
At the end, the Yakṣa was pleased and asked him to choose one brother to be restored. Yudhiṣṭhira did not choose selfishly. He chose Nakula, so that both Kuntī and Mādrī would each have one living son.
This story shows the spirit of 12.17. A mature mind may feel pain, but it does not become overpowered by pain. It preserves discrimination even when the situation is emotionally intense.
2. Story of Ruru and the Serpent
In the Mahābhārata, Ruru loved Pramadvarā deeply. Their marriage was arranged, and joy filled his heart.
Then tragedy struck.
Pramadvarā was bitten by a snake and fell lifeless. Ruru was shattered. Later, through divine arrangement, she was restored to life, but the shock left a deep mark on him.
His grief turned into hatred. He made a vow to kill snakes wherever he found them.
One day, Ruru saw an old harmless serpent and raised his weapon to kill it.
The serpent spoke.
It explained that not all serpents were guilty. Why should an innocent being be destroyed because of Ruru’s hatred toward another? The serpent’s words made him reflect.
Ruru realized that grief had become dveṣa. His pain had made him unjust.
He stopped.
This story shows how sorrow can turn into hatred when viveka is lost. It also shows the beginning of mastery: the moment we recognize that emotion is clouding judgment, we can step back.
3. Story of King Parīkṣit Hearing the Bhāgavata
King Parīkṣit once made a grave mistake. Tired, thirsty, and disturbed, he insulted a sage by placing a dead snake around his neck. The sage’s son cursed him: in seven days, he would die by snakebite.
When Parīkṣit heard the curse, he could have reacted in many ways. He could have become furious. He could have tried to punish the boy. He could have clung desperately to the kingdom. He could have drowned in fear.
Instead, he recognized the situation as a call to turn fully toward the highest truth.
He gave up attachment to the throne, sat on the banks of Gaṅgā, and listened to Śuka teach the Bhāgavata.
The remaining days of life became sacred.
This story reflects na śocati and na kāṅkṣati. Parīkṣit did not waste the little time left in grief or craving. He turned toward Bhagavān and used the crisis for liberation.
4. Story of Yayāti Returning the Borrowed Youth
King Yayāti was cursed with old age while still full of desire. Unable to bear the loss of youth, he asked his sons if one of them would exchange youth for his old age.
Pūru agreed.
With renewed youth, Yayāti enjoyed pleasures for a long time. He had power, opportunity, and the means to fulfill desire. But after long enjoyment, a realization arose: desire is not ended by feeding it.
He understood that craving does not become quiet by indulgence. Like fire fed with ghee, it only grows.
Finally, Yayāti returned the youth to Pūru and accepted old age. More importantly, he turned away from the illusion that enjoyment could complete him.
This story illustrates na kāṅkṣati. The mature mind sees the limitation of craving. It does not keep chasing objects in the hope of becoming complete.
5. Story of Khaṭvāṅga Giving Up Heavenly Reward
King Khaṭvāṅga once helped the devas in battle. Pleased with his service, they offered him a boon.
He asked how much time remained in his life.
The answer was startling: only a very short time.
In that moment, Khaṭvāṅga did not ask for heaven, pleasure, fame, kingdom, or extended enjoyment. He saw the urgency of truth.
He returned to earth, withdrew the mind from worldly pursuits, and turned it completely toward Bhagavān.
This story reflects śubhāśubhaparityāgī. Even heavenly reward, even puṇya-phala, is not the goal for the mature seeker. When the highest truth is seen as the goal, both pāpa and puṇya are no longer pursued as ends.
