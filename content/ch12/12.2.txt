1. Shloka
श्रीभगवानुवाच
मय्यावेश्य मनो ये मां नित्ययुक्ता उपासते ।
श्रद्धया परयोपेतास्ते मे युक्ततमा मताः ॥12.2॥
śrī-bhagavān uvāca
mayyāveśya mano ye māṁ nityayuktā upāsate ।
śraddhayā parayopetāste me yuktatamā matāḥ ॥12.2॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says: among the two groups, those who worship Me with minds absorbed in Me, with constant commitment and supreme faith, are considered by Me to be the most accomplished yogis.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa first answers Arjuna by referring to those who worship Bhagavān in saguṇa form. mayyāveśya manaḥ means fixing the mind on Bhagavān; here “Me” is saguṇa Kṛṣṇa, either as personal Bhagavān or as Viśvarūpa.
They are nityayuktāḥ — ever steadfast, constantly committed, not practicing in fits and starts. Their connection with Bhagavān is steady and sustained. They are also śraddhayā parayopetāḥ — endowed with supreme faith. This faith is needed because the fruit of such meditation is inner growth, purity, sensitivity, and refinement, which are not gross, visible results.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa then gives His direct conclusion: te me yuktatamā matāḥ — in My view, these devotees are the most accomplished in Yoga among the two groups mentioned by Arjuna. Both saguṇa and nirguṇa are required in a graded manner, but in this verse Kṛṣṇa explicitly glorifies the saguṇa upāsakas.
4. Story
In the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, the story of Prahlāda beautifully illustrates this teaching.
Prahlāda was born in the house of the asura king Hiraṇyakaśipu, who opposed Bhagavān Viṣṇu. Despite growing up in such an environment, Prahlāda’s mind was constantly absorbed in Bhagavān.
Even as a child, he remembered Bhagavān at all times — not out of fear or obligation, but out of deep connection.
When Hiraṇyakaśipu tried to turn him away from devotion, Prahlāda remained steady. He was subjected to many trials — thrown from heights, exposed to fire, and attacked in various ways.
Yet, throughout all this, his mind did not shift away from Bhagavān.
When asked where his strength came from, Prahlāda simply said it came from remembering Bhagavān.
His life shows what it means to have mayyāveśya manaḥ and śraddhayā parayā — a mind absorbed and faith that does not waver.
5. Impact to Current Life
One of the biggest challenges we face today is not lack of information, but lack of a steadily placed mind. Our attention keeps moving from one thing to another. This verse reminds us that depth comes only when we deliberately place the mind and keep it there.
We also tend to practice important things only occasionally — with inspiration one day and forgetfulness the next. nityayuktāḥ reminds us that steadiness matters. Growth comes through continuity, not through random intensity.
Finally, this verse highlights the role of śraddhā. Many of the most important changes in life are inward and not immediately measurable. If we expect only visible, instant results, we become impatient. We need trust to continue a meaningful pursuit long enough for inner refinement to take place.
6. Reflection
Do we consciously place the mind on what is truly important, or do we let it keep drifting?
Do we stay committed with śraddhā, or do we give up when results are not immediately visible?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“You know how when you really like something, you keep thinking about it again and again?
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying that some people keep their mind on Bhagavān like that all the time. And they really trust Bhagavān completely.
He says those people become very strong and steady.”
7B. Story
A boy loved playing cricket.
Even when he was not playing, he would think about it — how to improve, how to practice better.
Because of this, he kept getting better.
Another boy played sometimes, but got distracted easily. He did not improve much.
The difference was focus and consistency.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying something similar — when we keep our mind steady and stay committed, we grow strongly.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What do you like so much that you keep thinking about it?
Can you stay focused on something you want to learn?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: śrī-bhagavān uvāca = Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa said
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now begins His answer to Arjuna’s question about the two groups of devotees.
Step 2: First Group Identified
Phrase: mayyāveśya mano ye māṁ upāsate = those who, placing their mind in Me, worship Me
“Me” here is saguṇa Kṛṣṇa, either as personal Bhagavān or as Viśvarūpa. The essential point is that the mind is deliberately fixed upon Bhagavān.
Step 3: Nature of Commitment
Phrase: nityayuktāḥ = ever steadfast, constantly connected
These devotees are not occasional practitioners. They are steadily committed, sincerely and steadfastly engaged in this upāsanā.
Step 4: Role of Faith
Phrase: śraddhayā parayopetāḥ = endowed with supreme faith
They are endowed with deep śraddhā. This faith is needed because the fruit of saguṇa dhyānam is inner transformation — expansion, purity, sensitivity, and refinement — and these are not gross, visible results.
Step 5: Kṛṣṇa’s Evaluation
Phrase: te me yuktatamā matāḥ = they are regarded by Me as the most accomplished yogis
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa gives His opinion directly: among the two groups raised by Arjuna, these devotees are the most accomplished in Yoga.
Step 6: Scope of This Answer
Phrase: te me … matāḥ = in My view, they are so regarded
This verse gives Kṛṣṇa’s first answer. Both saguṇa and nirguṇa have their place in a graded spiritual life, but in this verse Kṛṣṇa explicitly glorifies the saguṇa upāsakas.

श्रीभगवान् उवाच । ये मयि मनः आवेश्य माम् नित्ययुक्ताः उपासते, परया श्रद्धया उपेताः, ते मे युक्ततमाः मताः ।
9E. Sandhi
मयि + आवेश्य = मय्यावेश्य
परया + उपेताः = परयोपेताः
उपेताः + ते = उपेतास्ते
9F. Samāsa
नित्ययुक्ताः = कर्मधारय समास
युक्ततमाः = तद्धित-प्रयुक्त superlative form
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
आवेश्य is a ल्यपन्त form.
उपेताः and मताः are क्तप्रत्ययान्त forms.
The verse uses a cluster of qualifying expressions to identify the saguṇa upāsakas precisely.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Hanumān (Rāmāyaṇa)
When Hanumān stood on the shores of the vast ocean, the task before him felt impossible. The waters stretched endlessly, the winds roared, and the goal — Laṅkā — was far beyond sight.
Around him stood other vānaras, each measuring their strength, each calculating how far they could go. Some could leap a short distance, some a little farther — but none could cross the entire ocean.
For a moment, even Hanumān stood quietly.
Then Jāmbavān gently reminded him of his true strength — not just physical strength, but his devotion to Rāma.
As Hanumān closed his eyes, his thoughts turned completely toward Bhagavān Rāma. He remembered Rāma’s face, Rāma’s purpose, Rāma’s trust in him.
That remembrance was not casual. His entire mind became absorbed.
In that moment, doubt disappeared.
With a powerful leap, he crossed the ocean. Even in Laṅkā — surrounded by danger, insult, and threat — his mind never left Rāma.
His courage, clarity, and success all flowed from that one thing: unwavering absorption in Bhagavān.
2. Story of Mīrā Bāī
Mīrā was born into royalty, surrounded by comfort, expectations, and responsibilities. But from a young age, her heart was already given — not to the world around her, but to Kṛṣṇa.
In the quiet corners of the palace, she would sing to Him, speak to Him, and treat Him as though He were present before her.
As she grew older, her devotion did not fade — it deepened.
But the world around her did not understand. Her family expected her to live like a queen, to follow traditions, to maintain royal dignity.
Her devotion was questioned, criticized, and even opposed.
At times, she was isolated. At times, she faced real danger.
But Mīrā did not step back.
Her faith was not dependent on comfort or approval. It did not weaken under pressure.
When she sang, she sang only for Kṛṣṇa. When she suffered, she still turned only to Kṛṣṇa.
For her, Bhagavān was not an idea. He was her constant presence.
Even in the midst of difficulty, her mind did not shift.
Her life shows what it means to remain steadily connected, with devotion sustained by unwavering trust.
3. Story of Dhruva (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Dhruva was just a young boy, but his heart carried a deep hurt.
When he approached his father, he was rejected and insulted. Confused and pained, he turned to his mother.
Instead of filling him with anger, she guided him toward Bhagavān.
“If you want something that cannot be taken away,” she said, “seek Bhagavān.”
Taking these words seriously, Dhruva left home and entered the forest — alone.
Under the guidance of Nārada, he began meditation.
At first, his mind was restless. He was young, inexperienced, and surrounded by the silence of the forest.
But slowly, through discipline and faith, something changed.
He reduced distractions. He focused his attention. He repeated the name of Bhagavān.
Days passed. Weeks passed.
His mind, which once wandered, became steady.
His attention, which once scattered, became absorbed.
Over time, his meditation became so deep that he was no longer aware of the world around him.
When Bhagavān finally appeared before him, Dhruva did not immediately open his eyes — because his mind was already fully absorbed in that very presence.
His journey shows how steady focus and faith transform a restless mind into a firm one.
4. Story of the Focused Student
A young student once approached his teacher and said, “I try to learn, but my mind keeps wandering. How do I gain real focus?”
The teacher listened quietly.
The next day, he gave the student a pot filled to the brim with water and said, “Carry this through the village and return. Not a single drop should spill.”
The village was crowded — people walking, vendors calling out, children playing, carts moving through narrow paths.
The student held the pot carefully and began walking.
Every step required attention. Every movement had to be deliberate.
He did not look around. He did not engage with anything else. His entire focus was on the pot.
When he returned, not a single drop had spilled.
The teacher asked, “What did you see in the village?”
The student replied, “Nothing. My attention was only on the water.”
The teacher smiled and said, “That is how your mind must be.”
The student understood that growth does not come from occasional enthusiasm. It comes from steadily placed attention.
5. Story of a Devotee in Daily Life
In a small town, there lived a simple shopkeeper.
He was not a scholar. He did not perform elaborate rituals. His life was ordinary — opening his shop in the morning, serving customers, managing accounts, and closing at night.
But there was something different about him.
Every morning, before opening his shop, he would sit quietly and remember Bhagavān.
Not as a routine, but as a moment of connection.
As the day went on, he attended to his work fully. But in between tasks, his mind would gently return to Bhagavān — again and again.
When problems came, he did not panic immediately. There was a pause — an inward steadiness.
Years passed.
People began to notice that he remained calm even in difficulty. Success did not make him restless. Loss did not disturb him deeply.
Someone once asked him, “How do you remain so steady?”
He smiled and said, “I try not to let my mind wander too far.”
He lived in the world, but his mind remained anchored.
This is not withdrawal from life. It is steady connection within life.
