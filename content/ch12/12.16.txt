1. Shloka
अनपेक्षः शुचिर्दक्ष उदासीनो गतव्यथः ।
सर्वारम्भपरित्यागी यो मद्भक्तः स मे प्रियः ॥12.16॥
anapekṣaḥ śucirdakṣa udāsīno gatavyathaḥ ।
sarvārambhaparityāgī yo madbhaktaḥ sa me priyaḥ ॥12.16॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the devotee who is free from dependence and expectation, pure, efficient, impartial, free from sorrow, and who has renounced all selfish undertakings, is dear to Him.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa continues describing the parā-bhakta. This verse focuses on the inner freedom and practical maturity of such a devotee.
The first word is anapekṣaḥ. This means one who is not emotionally dependent on external factors for happiness. Dependence expresses itself as expectation. When we depend on a thing, person, situation, result, or behavior, we naturally expect it to be a certain way. When that expectation is not fulfilled, disappointment and frustration arise. The parā-bhakta is free from this dependence, and therefore free from binding expectation.
Then comes śuciḥ — pure. This purity is not merely physical cleanliness, though external cleanliness has its place. It also includes purity of motive, purity of conduct, and purity of mind. The devotee’s inner life is not driven by manipulation, hidden selfishness, or crookedness.
The devotee is also dakṣaḥ — efficient, alert, capable, and resourceful. Spirituality is not carelessness or incompetence. A mature devotee is able to do what has to be done properly, intelligently, and at the right time.
Udāsīnaḥ means impartial. The devotee is not swept away by personal likes and dislikes. He can stand apart inwardly and respond with fairness. Gatavyathaḥ means one from whom mental pain or sorrow has gone. Since expectations and dependence have been loosened, the mind is not constantly wounded by life.
Finally, sarvārambha-parityāgī means one who has given up all selfish undertakings. This does not mean giving up all action. It means giving up ego-driven projects born of insecurity, ambition, possessiveness, or self-importance. Such a devotee may still act, serve, teach, guide, and work, but not from personal craving. Such a devotee is dear to Bhagavān.
4. Story
In the life of Śrī Candraśekharendra Sarasvatī, the sage of Kāñcī, we see a beautiful example of simplicity, purity, alertness, and freedom from expectation.
He lived with almost nothing. Though thousands revered him, he did not live like someone who depended on honor, comfort, possessions, or recognition. His food was simple. His movements were simple. His personal needs were few.
People from many backgrounds came to him — scholars, villagers, politicians, children, devotees, skeptics, and the distressed. He listened with attention. He did not respond from personal preference. Whether a person was rich or poor, famous or unknown, learned or simple, he gave attention according to what was needed.
There was also great dakṣatā in him. People sometimes imagine that a renunciate is detached because he is inactive or indifferent. But he was highly alert. He remembered details, guided institutions, protected traditions, encouraged learning, advised people in personal and spiritual matters, and acted with remarkable practical intelligence.
At the same time, he did not appear to carry personal anxiety. Things came; things went. Praise came; criticism came. Crowds came; solitude came. He remained inwardly light.
His life shows anapekṣatvam: freedom from dependence and expectation. It shows śaucam: purity and simplicity. It shows dakṣatā: alert competence. It shows udāsīnatā: impartiality. And it shows sarvārambha-parityāga: no selfish project, no personal empire-building, no ego-driven agenda.
He acted, but he did not act for himself.
5. Impact to Current Life
A large part of modern frustration comes from expectation. We expect children to behave a certain way, colleagues to appreciate us, friends to respond immediately, institutions to be fair, traffic to move quickly, and life to follow our plan. When these expectations fail, the mind becomes restless.
This verse does not ask us to become careless or passive. It asks us to notice emotional dependence. We can care without leaning. We can love without controlling. We can work without expecting the world to obey our script.
The word dakṣaḥ is also very relevant. Spiritual life is sometimes misunderstood as becoming vague or impractical. But Kṛṣṇa praises efficiency. A devotee should be alert, responsible, and capable.
The verse also helps in leadership and family life. Impartiality is needed whenever we deal with people. If we act only from likes and dislikes, we become unfair. If we are inwardly free, we can respond with clarity.
Finally, sarvārambha-parityāga asks us to examine our projects. Are we acting for dharma, service, and responsibility? Or are we driven by ego, insecurity, comparison, and the need to prove ourselves?
6. Reflection
Where do my expectations create repeated disappointment and frustration?
Are my actions guided by clarity and duty, or by ego-driven self-importance?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose you help your friend, but you expect your friend to praise you every time. If your friend forgets, you feel sad or angry.
Kṛṣṇa is saying that a good devotee does good things without always expecting something back.
A good devotee is also clean, careful, fair, and calm. Such a person does what is right, but does not keep saying, ‘What will I get?’”
7B. Story
A child helped clean the classroom.
The child arranged books, picked up pencils, and wiped the board.
At first, the child kept looking at the teacher, hoping the teacher would say, “Very good!”
But the teacher was busy and did not notice immediately.
The child felt a little upset.
Then another child said, “The classroom looks so nice now. Everyone can use it.”
The child smiled and thought, “I helped because it was good to help.”
Later the teacher noticed and thanked the child. But by then, the child had already felt happy inside.
That is what Kṛṣṇa is teaching — do the right thing carefully, without depending on praise.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Can you help someone without waiting for praise?
Can you do your work neatly and carefully because it is the right thing to do?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Freedom from Dependence
Phrase: anapekṣaḥ = free from expectation / dependence
The devotee is not emotionally dependent on external factors for happiness. Dependence produces expectation; expectation produces disappointment. Freedom from dependence gives freedom from frustration.
Step 2: Purity
Phrase: śuciḥ = pure
Purity includes external cleanliness, but more importantly purity of mind, motive, speech, and conduct. The devotee is not inwardly crooked or manipulative.
Step 3: Efficiency
Phrase: dakṣaḥ = efficient, capable, alert
The devotee is not careless. Spiritual maturity includes practical alertness and the capacity to do what has to be done properly.
Step 4: Impartiality
Phrase: udāsīnaḥ = impartial / inwardly uninvolved
The devotee is not carried away by likes and dislikes. He can stand apart inwardly and respond fairly.
Step 5: Freedom from Sorrow
Phrase: gatavyathaḥ = free from mental pain / sorrow
Since the devotee does not depend on external factors and does not live with binding expectations, the mind is not repeatedly wounded by disappointment.
Step 6: Renunciation of Selfish Undertakings
Phrase: sarvārambhaparityāgī = one who has renounced all selfish undertakings
This does not mean abandoning all action. It means giving up ego-driven, selfish, anxiety-born projects. The devotee may act vigorously, but not from personal craving or self-importance.
Step 7: Dear to Bhagavān
Phrase: yo madbhaktaḥ sa me priyaḥ = that devotee of Mine is dear to Me
The one who has these qualities is a true devotee. Such a person is dear to Bhagavān because the mind is free, pure, capable, impartial, and no longer driven by selfish beginnings.

9E. Sandhi
शुचिः + दक्षः = शुचिर्दक्षः
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga after इ changes to र् before the following soft consonant द्.
दक्षः + उदासीनः = दक्ष उदासीनः
Sandhi name: विसर्गलोपः / विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga is dropped before the following vowel उ.
सः + मे = स मे
Sandhi name: विसर्गलोपः; final visarga is dropped before the following consonant म्.
मत् + भक्तः = मद्भक्तः
Sandhi name: व्यञ्जनसन्धिः / जश्त्वसन्धिः; final त् becomes द् before the voiced consonant भ्.
9F. Samāsa
गतव्यथः = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: गता व्यथा यस्य सः — one whose sorrow has gone.
सर्वारम्भपरित्यागी = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष / बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: सर्वेषाम् आरम्भाणाम् परित्यागः यस्य सः — one who has renounced all undertakings, especially selfish or ego-driven undertakings.
मद्भक्तः = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: मम भक्तः — My devotee.
अनपेक्षः = नञ्-तत्पुरुष sense
Breakdown: न अपेक्षा यस्य / अपेक्षारहितः — one free from expectation.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
गत in गतव्यथः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from गम् धातुः, meaning “gone.”
भक्तः in मद्भक्तः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त form from भज् धातुः.
परित्यागी is formed with the त्यागिन् sense, meaning “one who renounces.”
All the descriptive terms are प्रथमा-एकवचनम् and qualify the implied devotee.
प्रियः is also प्रथमा-एकवचनम् and completes the statement “he is dear.”
10. More Stories
1. Story of Śrī Nārāyaṇa Guru and the Temple Consecration
In Kerala, there was a time when many people were denied dignity and access to worship because of birth-based social divisions.
Śrī Nārāyaṇa Guru saw this suffering clearly. But he did not respond with hatred. He did not begin an ego-driven movement to defeat one group and glorify another. His response came from purity, clarity, and concern for upliftment.
At Aruvippuram, he consecrated a Śiva liṅga.
This was a powerful act. Many people questioned him, because they believed only certain people had the right to perform such a consecration.
When challenged, Śrī Nārāyaṇa Guru is remembered for responding with calm intelligence and spiritual clarity. He did not become agitated. He did not speak with bitterness. He showed that worship of Bhagavān cannot be confined by narrow social arrogance.
His action was dakṣatā — skillful, alert, and timely. It was also śuciḥ — pure in motive. He did not act for personal fame or anger, but for dharma, dignity, and spiritual access.
This reflects sarvārambha-parityāga. Though he undertook great work, it was not a selfish undertaking. It was action free from egoistic ambition.
2. Story of Swāmī Tapovanam and the Student in the Himalayas
Swāmī Tapovanam lived in the Himalayas with great simplicity. The cold was intense, the surroundings were austere, and there were very few comforts.
One day, a student approached him, hoping to study Vedānta. The student was inspired, but not yet prepared for the discipline required. Life in the Himalayas was not easy. Food was simple. Walking was difficult. The climate was harsh. There was no room for luxury or complaint.
Swāmī Tapovanam did not entertain emotional dependence. He did not soften the path merely to satisfy the student’s comfort. Nor did he reject the student harshly.
He taught with clarity and expected discipline.
The student gradually saw that spiritual life was not about dramatic emotion or outer appearance. It required purity, steadiness, and freedom from unnecessary dependence.
Swāmī Tapovanam’s life itself was the teaching. He did not need many possessions. He did not launch restless projects. He did not depend on crowds or praise. When students came, he taught. When solitude came, he remained content.
This shows anapekṣaḥ and sarvārambha-parityāgī. He was free from expectation, yet not inactive. He acted only as required by dharma and teaching.
3. Story of Śrī Śāradā Devī Cooking for Devotees
Śrī Śāradā Devī lived with extraordinary simplicity. Many devotees came to her — some peaceful, some troubled, some immature, some deeply wounded by life.
One day, after a long and tiring day, people still came needing food, attention, and comfort. In an ordinary mind, expectation would arise: “Why do people keep coming? Why should I serve again? Why is there no rest?”
But Śrī Śāradā Devī did not relate to service in that way.
She cooked, served, listened, and cared with motherly patience. Her work was not dramatic, but it was pure. There was no demand for recognition. There was no complaint that people did not understand her burden.
Her purity was not merely ritual purity. It was purity of heart.
She did what had to be done, quietly and carefully. She was not careless. She was not sentimental in a weak way. She was practical, attentive, and inwardly free.
This reflects śuciḥ, dakṣaḥ, and gatavyathaḥ. There was purity, efficiency, and freedom from inner complaint.
4. Story of Swāmī Śivānanda and the Sick Visitor
At the āśrama in Ṛṣikesh, a sick and tired visitor once arrived needing help. In many places, such a person might have been seen as an inconvenience.
But Swāmī Śivānanda had the eye of service.
He noticed the person’s condition and responded immediately. Food, medicine, rest, and care were arranged. He did not merely give a lecture on spirituality while ignoring practical suffering. His spirituality expressed itself as alert action.
This was dakṣatā — practical efficiency.
At the same time, there was no sense of personal display. He did not act so that people would praise him as compassionate. He did not serve with the expectation that the visitor should become loyal to him. Service was simply what the situation required.
If someone praised him, he moved on. If someone criticized, he moved on. The work continued.
This shows anapekṣaḥ and dakṣaḥ together. Freedom from expectation did not make him dull. It made his action cleaner, quicker, and more useful.
5. Story of Ānandamayī Mā and the Grieving Devotee
A grieving devotee once came to Ānandamayī Mā, overwhelmed by personal sorrow. The person was not ready for philosophy. Words alone would not help.
Ānandamayī Mā received the devotee with quiet presence.
She did not become agitated by the grief. She did not push it away. She did not turn it into a personal burden. She allowed the person to sit, weep, and slowly become quiet in her presence.
There was deep compassion, but not emotional disturbance.
This is a subtle quality. Many people either become indifferent to another’s pain, or they get swallowed by it. Ānandamayī Mā showed another way — caring without losing inner steadiness.
Her presence gave comfort because she was not inwardly shaken. She was available, but not dependent. Loving, but not overwhelmed. Responsive, but not restless.
This reflects udāsīnaḥ and gatavyathaḥ. Here udāsīnaḥ does not mean cold indifference. It means inner poise, impartiality, and freedom from being dragged into disturbance.
