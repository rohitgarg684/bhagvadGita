1. Shloka
यस्मान्नोद्विजते लोको लोकान्नोद्विजते च यः ।
हर्षामर्षभयोद्वेगैर्मुक्तो यः स च मे प्रियः ॥12.15॥
yasmānnodvijate loko lokānnodvijate ca yaḥ ।
harṣāmarṣabhayodvegairmukto yaḥ sa ca me priyaḥ ॥12.15॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the devotee by whom the world is not disturbed, who is not disturbed by the world, and who is free from elation, intolerance, fear, and anxiety, is dear to Him.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa continues describing the parā-bhakta, the highest devotee, who has gone through the stages of bhakti-yoga and has gained inner freedom. This freedom is not about escaping the world physically. It is inner freedom from the regular emotional disturbances that make the mind a burden.
The first mark of such a devotee is that the world is not disturbed by him. This means he is sensitive, considerate, and aware of the feelings of others. He does not deliberately hurt people. He is ahiṁsaka in attitude. Unknowingly, someone may still feel hurt, but his intention and way of living are not harmful.
The second mark is that he is not disturbed by the world. People may criticize, insult, misunderstand, or behave unfairly. Still, he does not collapse inwardly. His heart is tender like a flower when dealing with others, but firm like a diamond when receiving difficult experiences.
Because he does not hurt others, he is free from guilt. Because he does not get hurt by others, he is free from inner wound. Hurt and guilt are two powerful sources of mental disturbance, and the parā-bhakta is free from both.
Bhagavān then mentions four inner disturbances: harṣa, amarṣa, bhaya, and udvega. Harṣa is over-excitement or elation that clouds discrimination. Amarṣa is intolerance, impatience, or envy when others excel. Bhaya is fear born of dependence on insecure things. Udvega is mental disturbance, anxiety, or sorrow caused by these inner reactions. The parā-bhakta is free from these; therefore he is dear to Bhagavān.
4. Story
In the life of Mahātmā Gāndhī, we see a powerful example of not disturbing the world and not being disturbed by the world.
Gāndhī faced criticism, insult, imprisonment, violence, and repeated misunderstanding. Many people opposed him strongly. Some mocked him, some attacked him, and some dismissed his methods as weakness.
Yet his central commitment was ahiṁsā. He did not want his freedom struggle to become a movement of hatred. He insisted that even when resisting injustice, one should not hate the person who commits injustice.
This was not passivity. Gāndhī opposed wrong firmly. He organized movements, accepted imprisonment, challenged unjust systems, and awakened millions. But he repeatedly taught that the method must not poison the heart. The struggle should not be driven by revenge.
When violence broke out among his own people, he was deeply pained. He fasted, prayed, and tried to awaken their conscience. He wanted freedom, but not at the cost of inner degradation.
This reflects the teaching of 12.15. The mature person does not disturb the world through hatred, aggression, or careless action. At the same time, he does not become inwardly shattered by insult, opposition, or suffering.
His heart is soft when dealing with others, but firm when receiving the blows of life.
5. Impact to Current Life
In modern life, we often disturb others without noticing it — through harsh words, impatience, online comments, anger at home, workplace pressure, sarcasm, or careless behavior. This verse asks us to become sensitive to the effect we have on people around us.
At the same time, we are easily disturbed by the world. A message, a criticism, a social media comment, a delay, a comparison, or a small disrespect can upset us for hours. Our mind becomes dependent on how others behave.
This verse gives a two-sided discipline. First, let me not become a source of disturbance to others. Second, let me not allow every external situation to disturb me.
It also warns us about emotional extremes. Happiness is natural, but over-excitement can cloud judgment. Disagreement is natural, but intolerance becomes poisonous. Caution is natural, but fear becomes bondage when we lean psychologically on insecure things. Anxiety becomes constant when the mind depends on what is unstable.
The teaching is not to become cold or emotionless. It is to become inwardly free, alert, considerate, and steady.
6. Reflection
Do others feel safe and undisturbed in my presence, or do my words and moods unsettle them?
Am I inwardly steady, or does every criticism, comparison, fear, and success disturb my mind?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose you are holding a glass of water. If someone bumps you, the water spills only if the glass is shaky.
Our mind is also like that. If the mind is very shaky, small things disturb us.
Kṛṣṇa is saying that a good devotee does not disturb others and also does not get disturbed easily. Such a person is kind to others and strong inside.”
7B. Story
A child was building a tower with blocks.
Another child accidentally bumped the table, and the tower fell.
At first, the child became angry and wanted to shout.
Then the teacher said, “Pause. Was it done on purpose?”
The child looked and saw that the other child was already sorry.
So the child took a deep breath and said, “It’s okay. Let’s build it again.”
This time, both children built the tower together.
The first child did not hurt the other child with angry words. And the child also did not stay upset for a long time.
That is what Kṛṣṇa is teaching — do not disturb others, and do not let every small thing disturb you.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Can you speak kindly even when something goes wrong?
What helps you calm down when you feel upset?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: The World Is Not Disturbed by Him
Phrase: yasmānnodvijate lokaḥ = by whom the world is not disturbed
The parā-bhakta is sensitive and considerate. He does not deliberately hurt others. He is ahiṁsaka in his attitude and conduct. People around him are not disturbed because of his presence, speech, or behavior.
Step 2: He Is Not Disturbed by the World
Phrase: lokānnodvijate ca yaḥ = and who is not disturbed by the world
The world may bring criticism, insult, difficulty, misunderstanding, or unpleasant experiences. Still, he does not get inwardly shaken. When handling others, he is soft like a flower; when receiving experiences, he is firm like a diamond.
Step 3: Freedom from Elation
Phrase: harṣa-muktaḥ = free from elation
Harṣa means over-excitement or elation. Happiness is not a problem, but when happiness becomes so intense that it clouds discrimination, it becomes harmful. The parā-bhakta does not allow even joy to rob him of viveka.
Step 4: Freedom from Amarṣa
Phrase: amarṣa-muktaḥ = free from intolerance / envy
Amarṣa means intolerance, impatience, or envy. It is the inability to appreciate another person’s excellence. The mature devotee is able to recognize good qualities in others without feeling diminished.
Step 5: Freedom from Fear
Phrase: bhaya-muktaḥ = free from fear
Bhaya comes when we psychologically lean on insecure things. Possessions, status, body, reputation, power, and relationships can all become sources of fear when we depend on them for inner security. The jñānī does not lean on them for self-worth.
Step 6: Freedom from Mental Disturbance
Phrase: udvega-muktaḥ = free from anxiety and disturbance
Udvega means mental disturbance, sorrow, agitation, or anxiety caused by these inner reactions. Since the parā-bhakta is free from harṣa, amarṣa, and bhaya, he is also free from the udvega they create.
Step 7: Dear to Bhagavān
Phrase: yaḥ sa ca me priyaḥ = such a one is dear to Me
Such a devotee is dear to Bhagavān because the mind is free from the disturbances of saṁsāra. This is the mark of parā-bhakti, advaita-bhakti, and jñāna-niṣṭhā.

हर्षामर्षभयोद्वेगैः = द्वन्द्व समासः
Breakdown: हर्षः च अमर्षः च भयम् च उद्वेगः च — by elation, intolerance/envy, fear, and anxiety.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
उद्विजते is लट्-लकारः, आत्मनेपदम्, from उद्-विज् धातुः, meaning “is disturbed” or “is agitated.”
मुक्तः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from मुच् धातुः, meaning “freed” or “released.”
यस्मात् and लोकात् are पञ्चमी-विभक्ति forms indicating “from whom” and “from the world.”
हर्षामर्षभयोद्वेगैः is तृतीया-बहुवचनम्, showing the factors from which the devotee is free.
The implied subject throughout is the parā-bhakta described in the sequence of verses.
12.15 Section 10: More Stories
1. Story of Śrī Ramana Maharṣi and the Cow Lakṣmī
At the āśrama of Śrī Ramana Maharṣi, the cow Lakṣmī was not treated as an ordinary animal wandering near the premises.
She would come near Bhagavān with affection and trust. Devotees noticed that he responded to her with the same quiet attention he gave to human visitors.
One day, Lakṣmī approached him with a kind of familiarity that surprised newcomers. She stood close, and he gently received her presence. There was no irritation, no dismissal, no sense that an animal was an interruption.
Around him, squirrels, dogs, monkeys, birds, and cows moved with unusual ease. They seemed safe.
Human beings too came in many states — grieving, argumentative, confused, proud, or desperate. He did not create fear in them. His presence did not disturb.
And when his own body later suffered illness and pain, he did not turn that suffering into agitation for others. He remained inwardly steady.
This story reflects both sides of 12.15: the world was not disturbed by him, and he was not disturbed by the world.
2. Story of Sant Kabīr and the Angry Listeners
Sant Kabīr often sang in language that cut through religious pride. He did not flatter people’s identities. He challenged hollow ritual, empty scholarship, and social arrogance.
Because of this, some people became angry.
Once, while Kabīr sang in a public space, people from different communities objected to his words. Some felt he was criticizing their customs. Some felt exposed. Voices rose. The atmosphere became tense.
Kabīr did not respond with fear. Nor did he soften the truth merely to please the crowd.
But he also did not speak from hatred.
His purpose was not to insult. His purpose was to awaken. His words were sharp, but his heart was not violent.
He continued singing of the one Bhagavān beyond labels, beyond pride, beyond outer divisions.
This is a subtle form of lokānnodvijate. The world’s criticism did not shake him. At the same time, he did not disturb the world through personal hatred. If people were disturbed, it was because truth challenged their ego — not because Kabīr wished to hurt them.
3. Story of Samartha Rāmadās and the Insult
Samartha Rāmadās, the great devotee of Bhagavān Rāma, travelled widely, taught devotion, and encouraged strength rooted in dharma.
During his travels, not everyone received him with respect. Some tested him. Some mocked ascetics. Some spoke harshly.
In one remembered incident, when insult came his way, Rāmadās did not collapse into hurt pride. He did not respond with an outburst to defend his status. Instead, he remained composed and used the moment as teaching.
For him, insult was not a personal wound to be nursed. It was an opportunity to see whether the mind was leaning on public respect.
He continued his work.
This reflects freedom from udvega. When the mind does not depend on praise for its stability, insult loses much of its power. Such a person may still correct what is wrong, but does not become inwardly shattered.
4. Story of Sant Sakhūbāī Remembering Viṭṭhala
Sant Sakhūbāī lived in a difficult household. Her devotion to Viṭṭhala was deep, but those around her did not always understand it.
There were days when she longed to go to Paṇḍharpur and join the devotees of Viṭṭhala. But household burdens and harsh treatment stood in her way.
One day, the longing became intense. Her heart cried for Viṭṭhala.
In the devotional tradition, Bhagavān Himself is said to have responded to her suffering, taking care of her duties so that she could go for darśana.
Whether read as miracle or bhakti-poetry, the heart of the story is clear: Sakhūbāī did not let bitterness become her identity. The world around her was harsh, but her mind found refuge in Bhagavān.
She did not disturb others through retaliation, and she did not let suffering destroy her devotion.
This reflects the flower-and-diamond quality: soft in heart, strong in endurance.
5. Story of Śrī Śāradā Devī and the Difficult Devotee
Many kinds of people came to Śrī Śāradā Devī — gentle devotees, troubled seekers, confused householders, and people carrying guilt or emotional pain.
Once, a devotee came in a disturbed state, speaking restlessly and seeking comfort. An ordinary person might have become impatient or judgmental.
Śrī Śāradā Devī received the person with motherly calm.
She listened. She did not frighten the person with harshness. She did not make them feel rejected because of their weakness. Her presence itself gave safety.
Yet she was not inwardly shaken by the person’s distress. She did not become agitated. She did not absorb the disturbance as her own. She remained steady, compassionate, and clear.
This is the quality praised in 12.15. Others were not disturbed by her presence; they felt protected. And she was not disturbed by the pain and confusion others brought to her.
