1. Shloka
तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिर्मौनी सन्तुष्टो येन केनचित् ।
अनिकेतः स्थिरमतिर्भक्तिमान्मे प्रियो नरः ॥12.19॥
tulyanindāstutirmaunī santuṣṭo yena kenacit ।
aniketaḥ sthiramatirbhaktimānme priyo naraḥ ॥12.19॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the devotee who is the same in criticism and praise, measured in speech, content with whatever comes, free from attachment to residence, and firm in Self-knowledge, is dear to Him.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa continues describing the parā-bhakta. In this verse, the qualities are very practical and deeply inward.
The devotee is tulya-nindā-stutiḥ — the same in criticism and praise. Praise does not inflate the person, and criticism does not break the person. This does not mean praise and criticism are not heard. They may be heard, evaluated, and responded to if needed. But the person’s inner worth does not depend on public opinion.
The devotee is maunī. This does not necessarily mean one who has taken a vow of total silence. It means a person of measured, disciplined, limited speech. Such a devotee does not speak unnecessarily, impulsively, or hurtfully. Speech becomes thoughtful, useful, and restrained.
The devotee is santuṣṭaḥ yena kenacit — content with whatever comes. This does not mean carelessness or lack of effort. It means the person does not make inner peace depend on perfect external arrangements. Whatever comes by Bhagavān’s order is received with balance.
The devotee is aniketaḥ. Literally, this can mean “homeless,” but the deeper sense here is freedom from attachment to residence, possession, and fixed security. A person may live in a house, but inwardly should not be bound by the idea, “Only this place is my safety and identity.”
Finally, the devotee is sthiramatiḥ — one whose understanding is firm. This is not temporary inspiration or emotional devotion alone. The mind is steady because the knowledge of the Self is firm. Such a bhaktimān naraḥ — such a devoted person — is dear to Bhagavān.
4. Story
Sadāśiva Brahmendra was a great saint known for his deep absorption in the Self.
He wandered freely, often unaware of the outer world. He did not live by social expectations, possessions, or fixed identity. His life itself seemed to say that the Self is not confined to a house, role, or public image.
One day, while absorbed inwardly, he wandered into a royal camp. The guards and attendants were startled. They did not understand his state. To them, he looked like someone who had violated ordinary boundaries and customs.
The ruler became angry.
In some traditional accounts, Sadāśiva Brahmendra was severely punished. But what is remembered most is not the outer event; it is his inner stillness. He did not protest in anger. He did not defend his dignity. He did not curse those who misunderstood him.
He remained absorbed.
Later, when the ruler realized that this was no ordinary wandering man, but a great saint, remorse arose. Reverence replaced anger.
For Sadāśiva Brahmendra, neither insult nor later reverence seems to have altered his inner state. When he was misunderstood, he was silent. When he was honored, he remained silent. Praise and blame passed like wind across the sky.
This story beautifully reflects tulya-nindā-stutiḥ, maunī, aniketaḥ, and sthiramatiḥ. His silence was not weakness; it arose from fullness. His homelessness was not poverty; it arose from freedom. His steadiness was not dullness; it arose from firm knowledge.
5. Impact to Current Life
Today, praise and criticism control many minds. A compliment can make us feel important; one negative comment can disturb us for hours. Social media has made this even stronger. Likes, shares, ratings, reviews, and public approval easily become emotional food.
This verse asks us to grow out of that dependence. We may learn from feedback, but we need not live by praise or die by criticism.
The teaching on speech is equally important. Much disturbance in life comes from unnecessary speech — gossip, reaction, exaggeration, sarcasm, complaint, and impulsive messages. Maunī reminds us that spiritual maturity includes speech discipline. Silence is not only not speaking; it is also knowing when, how, and why to speak.
Contentment with whatever comes is also very relevant. We often say, “I can be peaceful only if the house, job, family, health, and finances are exactly as I want.” This verse points to a deeper peace that does not wait for perfect conditions.
Aniketaḥ is powerful for modern life. We may own a home, use comforts, and care for family, but we need not make them our identity. Bhagavān gives; Bhagavān takes; what comes is received, what goes is returned.
6. Reflection
How much does my peace depend on praise, approval, and being seen positively by others?
Can I speak less impulsively and receive life more as prasāda?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Sometimes people praise us and say, ‘Very good!’ Sometimes people criticize us and say, ‘This is not good.’
Kṛṣṇa is saying that we should not become too proud when praised or too sad when criticized.
He also says we should speak carefully. We do not have to say everything that comes into our mind. A good devotee speaks kindly, speaks only what is useful, and stays happy with what is available.”
7B. Story
A child brought lunch to school.
One friend said, “Your lunch looks so tasty!”
The child felt happy.
Another child said, “I don’t like that food.”
The child started feeling bad.
Then the teacher said, “Pause. One person praised it. One person did not. But your lunch is still your lunch. Eat with gratitude.”
The child smiled and ate peacefully.
Later, during class, the child wanted to say something unkind about another student. But then the child remembered: “I should speak only what is kind and useful.”
So the child stayed quiet.
That day, the child learned two things: do not become too excited by praise or too sad by criticism, and speak carefully.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What happens in your mind when someone praises you?
Can you pause before saying something unkind?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Sameness in Criticism and Praise
Phrase: tulya-nindā-stutiḥ = one for whom criticism and praise are equal
The mature devotee does not become inflated by praise or wounded by criticism. Praise and blame may be useful as information, but they do not define the person’s inner worth.
Step 2: Measured Speech
Phrase: maunī = one of measured speech
Maunī here means a person of limited, disciplined, thoughtful speech. It is not necessarily absolute silence. The devotee avoids unnecessary, impulsive, and harmful speech.
Step 3: Contentment with Whatever Comes
Phrase: santuṣṭo yena kenacit = content with whatever comes
The devotee is content with what comes by Bhagavān’s order. This does not prevent effort, but it prevents complaint, dependence, and emotional agitation over external arrangements.
Step 4: Freedom from Attachment to Residence
Phrase: aniketaḥ = not attached to any fixed abode
This does not merely mean physically homeless. The deeper point is freedom from possessiveness and psychological dependence on house, place, and external security. The person may live somewhere, but does not bind the mind with “this alone is mine.”
Step 5: Firm Understanding
Phrase: sthiramatiḥ = one whose understanding is firm
The devotee has firm knowledge of the Self. The mind is steady because the intellect is clear. This is not casual devotion, but mature devotion supported by steady knowledge.
Step 6: Dear to Bhagavān
Phrase: bhaktimānme priyo naraḥ = such a devoted person is dear to Me
The devotee described here has devotion, restraint, contentment, inner freedom, and firm knowledge. Such a person is dear to Bhagavān because there is no inner dependence on praise, possession, speech, or security.

9E. Sandhi
निन्दा + स्तुतिः = निन्दास्तुतिः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final आ remains before स् without change.
तुल्य + निन्दास्तुतिः = तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final अ joins with न् without special phonetic change.
तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिः + मौनी = तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिर्मौनी
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga after इ changes to र् before the soft consonant म्.
सन्तुष्टः + येन = सन्तुष्टो येन
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final अः becomes ओ before the semivowel य्.

Sandhi name: संयोगसन्धिः; final न् joins with the following म् in continuous pronunciation.

9F. Samāsa
तुल्यनिन्दास्तुतिः = बहुव्रीहि समासः with द्वन्द्व inside
Breakdown: निन्दा च स्तुतिः च = निन्दास्तुती; तुल्ये निन्दास्तुती यस्य सः — one for whom criticism and praise are equal.
अनिकेतः = नञ्-तत्पुरुष / बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: न केतः / न निश्चितं केतनं यस्य सः — one who has no fixed dependence on residence.
स्थिरमतिः = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: स्थिरा मतिः यस्य सः — one whose understanding is firm.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
सन्तुष्टः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from सम्-तुष् धातुः, meaning “contented.”
मौनी is formed from मौन with इनि/इन् sense, meaning “one endowed with silence” or “one of restrained speech.”
भक्तिमान् is formed with मतुप्-प्रत्ययः, meaning “one who possesses bhakti.”
येन केनचित् is an indefinite expression in तृतीया-विभक्ति, meaning “with anything whatsoever.”
All major descriptive terms are प्रथमा-एकवचनम् and qualify नरः.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Dattātreya and King Yadu
In the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, King Yadu once saw an unusual wandering sage.
The sage looked free, joyful, and untouched by the ordinary burdens of life. He had no royal protection, no fixed palace, no visible possessions, and no anxious planning. Yet he seemed inwardly full.
Yadu was puzzled.
“How can you be so happy,” he asked, “when you move through the world without attachment?”
The sage was Dattātreya.
Dattātreya explained that he had learned from the whole world. Earth taught patience. Air taught freedom from attachment. Sky taught vastness. Water taught purity. Fire taught brightness and detachment. Many beings and situations became his teachers.
He was not bound to one place or one arrangement. Wherever he went, life taught him. Whatever came, he received. Whatever went, he allowed to go.
This is aniketaḥ and santuṣṭo yena kenacit. The world itself became his āśrama, because his mind was not imprisoned by possession.
His contentment did not come from having everything. It came from needing very little and learning from everything.
2. Story of Paṭṭinattār and the Needle
Paṭṭinattār was once a wealthy merchant. His ships brought goods, his house was full, and society respected him.
Then one day, a small incident shattered his old understanding.
A message came to him connected with a simple needle — a reminder that not even a needle without an eye can be taken after death. The meaning struck him deeply. All the wealth he had gathered, all the status he had enjoyed, all the security he had built — none of it could be carried beyond death.
He did not merely feel sad for a day and return to business.
He renounced.
Leaving behind wealth, home, and status, he became a wandering saint. People who once honored him may have thought him mad. Some praised him as a saint; others mocked him as one who had lost everything.
But Paṭṭinattār had seen through the game.
His home was no longer his identity. Praise and blame no longer had the old hold. He became aniketaḥ — free from dependence on residence and possession — and sthiramatiḥ, firm in the clarity that the Self is not enriched by wealth nor diminished by poverty.
3. Story of Māṇikkavācakar and the King’s Horses
Māṇikkavācakar served as a minister to the Pāṇḍya king. Once he was entrusted with royal wealth to buy horses for the kingdom.
On the way, he encountered the presence of Bhagavān Śiva in a transforming way. His heart was seized by devotion. Instead of buying horses, he used the wealth for building and serving a temple.
When the king heard this, anger rose. To the king, it looked like betrayal. Māṇikkavācakar was summoned and accused.
Praise turned into blame. Honor turned into humiliation.
But Māṇikkavācakar remained absorbed in Bhagavān. He did not defend himself with panic. He did not become broken by public dishonor.
The story continues with Bhagavān Śiva intervening in a wondrous way, showing that what appeared as worldly failure was in truth divine play.
Māṇikkavācakar’s steadiness shows tulya-nindā-stutiḥ. When honored as minister, he did not lose himself. When blamed as a failure, he did not lose himself. His mind was with Bhagavān.
4. Story of Kāraikkāl Ammaiyār Leaving Beauty and Home
Kāraikkāl Ammaiyār lived as a devoted woman in a household. One day, a mango came into her home, and through a series of events connected with devotion to Bhagavān Śiva, her husband realized that she was not an ordinary person.
Instead of seeing her as a wife alone, he began to revere her as divine.
For Kāraikkāl Ammaiyār, this became a turning point. She did not cling to household identity, beauty, marriage-status, or social role. Her heart belonged to Bhagavān Śiva.
She prayed to be freed from ordinary bodily beauty so that no worldly identity would bind her. She left the conventional life and became one of the great devotees of Śiva.
Her story is not about rejection born of bitterness. It is about freedom from identity and possession.
She became aniketā in spirit — not bound by house, body, beauty, or social definition. Praise and blame from the world could not measure her life. Her mind was firm in Bhagavān.
5. Story of Ṛṣabhadeva as an Avadhūta
In the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, Ṛṣabhadeva is described as one who had already fulfilled his responsibilities and then moved through the world as an avadhūta.
He had been a king, a teacher, and a guide. But after instructing his sons, he gave up royal life and wandered freely, unattached to residence, possession, status, and public image.
People did not always understand him.
Some mocked him. Some insulted him. Some treated him as though he were mad. To ordinary eyes, a king who had given up everything and moved without concern for appearance seemed strange.
But Ṛṣabhadeva remained untouched.
Praise did not attract him. Insult did not disturb him. He did not speak to defend his status. He did not try to prove his greatness. His silence was not helplessness; it came from inner fullness.
He had no fixed dependence on home, role, or recognition. The world could call him noble or mad, king or wanderer — none of these labels altered his understanding.
This story beautifully reflects tulya-nindā-stutiḥ, maunī, aniketaḥ, and sthiramatiḥ. One who is established inwardly does not need the world’s praise and is not injured by the world’s criticism.
