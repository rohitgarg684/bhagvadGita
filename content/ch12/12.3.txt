1. Shloka
ये त्वक्षरमनिर्देश्यमव्यक्तं पर्युपासते ।
सर्वत्रगमचिन्त्यं च कूटस्थमचलंध्रुवम् ॥12.3॥
ye tvakṣaramanirdeśyamavyaktaṁ paryupāsate ।
sarvatragamacintyaṁ ca kūṭasthamacalaṁdhruvam ॥12.3॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now speaks of those who meditate upon the imperishable, indefinable, unmanifest, all-pervading, inconceivable, changeless, unmoving, and eternal reality.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now introduces the second group mentioned by Arjuna. These are not devotees who worship Bhagavān in a form. They meditate upon akṣaram — the imperishable reality, which is nirguṇa brahman.
Kṛṣṇa then describes this reality through several important words. It is avyaktam — not available for any sense perception. It is acintyam — not available as a mental concept. It is anirdeśyam — not something that can be clearly pointed out or described through words. In this way, we are asked to understand that this reality is not available as an object.
Because it is formless, it is sarvatragam — all-pervading. Whatever has form has a boundary, and whatever has a boundary is limited to one place. But that which is free from form is not spatially limited. Therefore it is also acalam — unmoving.
It is further described as kūṭastham and dhruvam. This means it is changeless and constant. It does not undergo the modifications that all forms undergo. Swāmī Paramārthānanda explains kūṭastham through the example of an anvil: many changes take place upon it, but it itself remains unchanged. In the same way, this reality remains changeless while all changing experiences appear upon it.
Since this reality cannot be objectified, meditation upon it is unique. It can be meditated upon only as the very subject, the meditator himself or herself. Therefore nirguṇa brahma upāsanā is really ātma-dhyānam.
4. Story
In the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, there is a profound dialogue between Yājñavalkya and Maitreyī.
Yājñavalkya had decided to leave home and dedicate himself completely to the life of renunciation. Before departing, he told his wife Maitreyī that he would divide his wealth.
Maitreyī listened quietly and then asked a startling question: “If all this wealth were mine, would it make me immortal?”
Yājñavalkya replied gently, “No. Wealth can make life comfortable, but it cannot give immortality.”
Maitreyī did not ask for gold, land, or comfort. She said, “Then teach me that by which immortality is gained.”
Yājñavalkya was pleased. He began teaching her the truth of the Self. He explained that everything in life is loved not for its own sake, but for the sake of the Self. Husband, wife, wealth, and the world are meaningful only because the Self is there.
Then he led her into subtler and subtler understanding. The Self cannot be seen as an object. It cannot be grasped as one more thing among things. It is not something outside us to be pointed at. It is that because of which all seeing, hearing, and knowing take place.
Maitreyī found the teaching difficult because it did not present something visible or tangible. But that was exactly the point. The highest reality is not an object.
This is the spirit of 12.3. The truth being meditated upon is avyaktam, anirdeśyam, and acintyam.
5. Impact to Current Life
We are normally comfortable only with what we can see, touch, measure, and define. If something is not available to the senses or cannot be clearly described, we tend to feel it is vague or unreal.
This verse challenges that habit. It reminds us that the highest reality is not something available in the same way as ordinary objects. If we depend only on names and forms, we remain tied to what is changing.
Much of our insecurity comes from identifying only with changing things — body, role, position, possessions, and relationships. As these change, we feel shaken. This verse points us toward appreciating that behind all change there is a changeless basis.
Even if we are not ready for full contemplation of nirguṇa brahman, this teaching helps us loosen our dependence on what is merely visible and changing.
6. Reflection
Do I assume that only what I can see and define is real?
Can I begin to appreciate that the deepest truth may not be available as an object?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“You know how we cannot see the air, but we still know it is there? We feel it when the wind blows, and we use it every moment when we breathe.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying that some people think about Bhagavān in a way like that — not as a person they can see, but as something that is everywhere, never changes, and is always present.”
7B. Story
A child once asked, “Where is the air?”
The parent smiled and said, “It is everywhere.”
The child looked around the room and said, “But I can’t see it.”
So the parent opened a window. A cool breeze came in and touched the child’s face.
“Can you feel it now?” the parent asked.
“Yes,” the child said. “But I still can’t see it.”
The parent replied, “That is how some things are. We may not see them, but they are still real.”
Then the parent pointed to a balloon, a moving curtain, and the child’s own breath.
“Air is everywhere. You may not see it directly, but you know it is there.”
In the same way, some people think about Bhagavān as something we cannot see with our eyes, but which is always present everywhere.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Can you think of something real that you cannot see directly?
How do you know it is there?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: ye tu … paryupāsate = but those who meditate
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now introduces the second group mentioned by Arjuna. These are the devotees who take to another form of upāsanā.
Step 2: Object of Meditation
Phrase: akṣaram = the imperishable
The object of their meditation is akṣaram — that which does not perish or undergo destruction. This is nirguṇa brahman.
Step 3: Not Available to Senses and Words
Phrase: anirdeśyamavyaktaṁ = indefinable and unmanifest
This reality is avyaktam — not available for sense perception. It is anirdeśyam — not something that can be clearly pointed out or described through words.
Step 4: Not Available as a Mental Object
Phrase: acintyaṁ = inconceivable
It is not available even as a mental concept. We cannot grasp it as an object of thought in the usual way.
Step 5: Free from Spatial Limitation
Phrase: sarvatragam = all-pervading
Because it has no form, it has no boundary. Therefore it is present everywhere and is not confined to any one location.
Step 6: Changeless and Constant
Phrase: kūṭasthamacalaṁdhruvam = changeless, unmoving, and constant
It is kūṭastham — unchanged like an anvil that remains the same while changes happen upon it. It is acalam — unmoving, because the all-pervading cannot move from one place to another. It is dhruvam — constant and eternal.

9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
अनिर्देश्यम्, अव्यक्तम्, सर्वत्रगम्, अचिन्त्यम्, कूटस्थम्, अचलम्, ध्रुवम् — all are adjectives in neuter accusative singular qualifying अक्षरम्.
पर्युपासते is an आत्मनेपदी verb in the present tense.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Naciketā (Kaṭha Upaniṣad)
Naciketā was still a boy when he saw something that troubled him deeply. His father was performing a sacrifice and giving away old, weak cows in charity — gifts that had lost all real value.
A question arose in Naciketā’s mind: “If charity is to be sacred, how can it be done like this?”
Again and again he asked his father, “To whom will you give me?” Irritated, the father finally said in anger, “I give you to Yama!”
Naciketā took the words seriously and went to the abode of Yama, the lord of death. Yama was not there, and the boy waited for three days without complaint.
When Yama returned, he offered the boy three boons. With the third boon, Naciketā asked the deepest question of all: “What happens after death? Some say the person exists, some say not. I want to know that truth.”
Yama tried to distract him. He offered long life, wealth, heavenly pleasures, music, and power. But Naciketā refused them all.
“These are temporary,” he said. “Tell me that which does not perish.”
Only then did Yama teach him about the Self — that which is beyond death, beyond change, beyond objectification.
Naciketā was not interested in what comes and goes. He wanted akṣaram — the imperishable.
2. Story of Śvetaketu and the Salt in Water (Chāndogya Upaniṣad)
Śvetaketu returned home after years of study, proud of his learning. Seeing this, his father Uddālaka Āruṇi decided to teach him something deeper.
One day, he gave Śvetaketu a lump of salt and asked him to place it in water and return the next morning.
When Śvetaketu came back, the salt had dissolved.
His father said, “Bring me the salt.”
Śvetaketu searched the pot but could not find it.
Then Uddālaka asked him to taste the water from the top. “Salty,” he said. From the middle? “Salty.” From the bottom? “Salty.”
The father smiled. “You do not see the salt, but it is present throughout.”
In the same way, the deepest reality is not always available to our eyes. It may not stand before us as an object, yet it pervades everything.
Śvetaketu began to understand that what is most real need not always be visible.
This beautifully reflects avyaktam and sarvatragam — unmanifest and all-pervading.
3. Story of King Janaka
King Janaka ruled a great kingdom, but he was no ordinary king. Beneath the responsibilities of kingship there was in him a deep steadiness born of Self-knowledge.
One day, while listening to spiritual teaching, a sudden commotion broke out. A part of the palace had caught fire. Courtiers ran anxiously, servants shouted, and many students in the assembly grew restless.
Some rushed out to protect their belongings.
But Janaka remained seated.
Someone asked him in amazement, “O King, your palace is burning. Why are you still sitting here calmly?”
Janaka replied, “Nothing that is truly mine is burning.”
This was not carelessness. He was not denying the event. He was seeing clearly that the changing world, however important in transaction, was not his essential Self.
He had understood the changeless basis behind changing events.
That is why he could remain established in what is kūṭastham and dhruvam even while change unfolded around him.
4. Story of Bhagavān Ramana Maharṣi
When Venkataraman, later known as Bhagavān Ramana Maharṣi, was still a teenager, an intense fear of death suddenly arose in him one day.
He was alone. No one had threatened him. No illness had struck. Yet the certainty came: “I am going to die.”
Instead of running away from the thought, he turned inward.
He lay down and enacted death deliberately. He stiffened the body, held the breath, and asked within himself: “This body dies. But am I the body? What is it that is aware of this?”
His attention moved away from the body and mind toward the silent awareness that remained present even when the body was treated as dead.
In that direct inward turning, something became clear: the real “I” is not the body, not the changing personality, but the awareness because of which all of this is known.
That recognition transformed his life.
This story reflects how the reality being sought is not an object “out there.” It is the very subject — not seen, but that because of which everything is seen.
5. Story of Śrī Rāmakṛṣṇa and Totāpurī
Śrī Rāmakṛṣṇa’s heart overflowed with devotion to Kālī. For him, Bhagavān in form was living and immediate. He would speak, sing, and weep in devotion.
Then Totāpurī, a renunciate established in nirguṇa contemplation, came to Dakṣiṇeśvar.
He saw in Rāmakṛṣṇa an extraordinary spiritual intensity, but he also saw that the mind was still moving toward the divine form.
Totāpurī told him, “Go beyond name and form.”
Rāmakṛṣṇa tried, but each time the radiant form of Kālī would arise before his mind.
Then Totāpurī instructed him with great force to cut through even that form in meditation.
Rāmakṛṣṇa turned inward again, and this time the mind rose beyond all form into the experience of the formless.
For days he remained absorbed in that state.
This story shows that devotion to form and contemplation of the formless are not enemies. But 12.3 is specifically about that second movement — meditation upon the formless, unobjectifiable reality.
