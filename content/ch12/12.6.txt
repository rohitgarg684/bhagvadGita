1. Shloka
ये तु सर्वाणि कर्माणि मयि संन्यस्य मत्पराः ।
अनन्येनैव योगेन मां ध्यायन्त उपासते ॥12.6॥
ye tu sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya matparāḥ ।
ananyenaiva yogena māṁ dhyāyanta upāsate ॥12.6॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa describes those who dedicate all actions to Him, keep Him as the primary goal, and meditate upon Him with undivided commitment.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now comes down one step in the spiritual ladder. After discussing the highest level of nirguṇa contemplation, He now presents Viśvarūpa upāsanā as the next level for those not yet ready for the subtlest jñāna-yoga sādhana. This is still a powerful spiritual discipline, but it is more accessible because it gives the mind a vast and sacred form of Bhagavān to relate to.
The first instruction is sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya — dedicating all actions to Bhagavān. Here sannyāsa does not mean renouncing action. It means offering or dedicating action. When we perform an action, we do what is to be done, and once the action is completed, it becomes part of the universe. The result then comes through the laws of karma, which are understood as the hands of Viśvarūpa īśvara. Therefore, dedication means an inner attitude of offering the action and being ready to receive the result without grumbling, murmuring, cursing, or resistance.
The second expression is matparāḥ — keeping Bhagavān as the primary goal. Even while doing actions in the world, we should remember that the purpose of this discipline is spiritual growth. This level is not meant for getting lost in worldly activity; it is meant to qualify the mind for the next step, which is nirguṇa īśvara jñānam.
The third expression is ananyenaiva yogena — with an undistracted spiritual commitment. We may be involved in many duties, responsibilities, family situations, and social roles, but we should not forget the larger journey. Just as a traveler may get down at intermediate stations but must not forget the final destination, we may transact with the world without forgetting the spiritual goal.
Finally, māṁ dhyāyanta upāsate means that these devotees meditate upon Bhagavān. The meditation here is not merely occasional prayer, but Viśvarūpa upāsanā supported by a life of dedication, receptivity, and remembrance.
4. Story
In the Rāmāyaṇa, Hanumān’s mission to Laṅkā beautifully illustrates this teaching.
When Sītā was discovered to be in Laṅkā, the vānaras stood before the ocean, wondering who could cross it. The distance was enormous, and the task was dangerous. Hanumān was reminded of his own strength by Jāmbavān, but even that strength was not merely physical. It was awakened by remembering Rāma.
Before he leapt, Hanumān did not think, “This is my achievement.” His mind turned to Bhagavān Rāma. The mission was not for personal glory, not for name, not for reward. It was an offering.
As he flew across the ocean, met obstacles, entered Laṅkā, searched through the city, and finally found Sītā, every action was performed for Rāma. Even when he stood in Rāvaṇa’s court, surrounded by danger, he did not become self-centered.
When asked who he was, he identified himself as Rāma’s servant.
This is sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya in living form. Every action became an offering. The goal was not personal success, but service to Bhagavān.
Hanumān also shows matparatvam. Rāma remained the center. Even while acting intensely in the world, he never lost the destination.
5. Impact to Current Life
Many of us are active all day — working, managing family responsibilities, solving problems, responding to messages, handling money, planning, and reacting to situations. But activity by itself can make the mind scattered and extroverted. This verse gives us a way to spiritualize action without abandoning life.
When we dedicate actions to Bhagavān, we stop treating every result as a personal burden. We still act carefully and responsibly, but we learn to accept the result as coming through a larger order. This reduces anxiety, complaint, and resistance.
The verse also reminds us not to forget the bigger purpose. In modern life, we can become so absorbed in intermediate stations — career, possessions, social status, comfort — that we forget the larger journey of inner growth.
This teaching helps us live actively without losing spiritual direction. We work, serve, relate, and decide — but with the attitude that all actions are offered, all results are received, and spiritual growth remains the main goal.
6. Reflection
When I act, do I carry the whole burden personally, or do I offer the action to Bhagavān?
In the middle of responsibilities, do I remember the larger purpose of spiritual growth?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose you help your parents clean the house. You can do it thinking, ‘Why should I do this?’ Or you can think, ‘I am helping with love.’
The action may be the same, but the feeling changes.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying that we can do our actions and offer them to Bhagavān. That means we do our best, and then accept the result calmly.”
7B. Story
A child made a birthday card for a grandparent.
The child worked carefully — choosing colors, drawing flowers, and writing a message.
When the card was done, the child did not keep it and say, “This is mine.” The child happily gave it.
Even if the drawing was not perfect, the love behind it made it beautiful.
In the same way, Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that we can do our work and offer it with love.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Can you do one action today as an offering?
How do you feel when you do something with love instead of complaint?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: ye tu = but those
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now introduces another group of devotees. This comes after the discussion of the higher and more difficult nirguṇa contemplation. Here He presents a lower rung in the ladder, one that involves Viśvarūpa upāsanā supported by dedicated action.
Step 2: Dedication of All Actions
Phrase: sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya = dedicating all actions unto Me
Here sannyasya does not mean giving up action. It means offering or dedicating all actions to Bhagavān. When an action is completed, it enters the universe and its result is shaped by the laws of karma. For the devotee, those laws are the hands of Bhagavān. Therefore, dedication means doing the action properly and receiving the result with inner receptivity.
Step 3: Bhagavān as the Primary Goal
Phrase: matparāḥ = keeping Me as the supreme / primary goal
While practicing this offering of action, we should remember the larger spiritual aim. The purpose is not merely worldly success but qualification for the higher step, nirguṇa īśvara jñānam.
Step 4: Undistracted Spiritual Orientation
Phrase: ananyenaiva yogena = by an undistracted spiritual discipline
Even while engaging in worldly duties, we should not become distracted from the final goal. Responsibilities may increase, and activities may multiply, but the inner direction should remain clear.
Step 5: Meditation upon Bhagavān
Phrase: māṁ dhyāyanta upāsate = meditating upon Me, they worship
These devotees meditate upon Bhagavān through Viśvarūpa upāsanā. Their meditation is supported by a life in which actions are offered and results are received as part of Bhagavān’s order.
Step 6: The Teaching
Phrase: sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya … māṁ dhyāyanta upāsate = dedicating all actions to Me, they meditate upon Me
The teaching is that spiritual growth is possible while living an active life. We need not abandon action. We transform action through dedication, keep Bhagavān as the goal, avoid distraction, and practice meditation upon Viśvarūpa īśvara.

9E. Sandhi
अनन्येन + एव = अनन्येनैव
Sandhi name: वृद्धिसन्धिः; final अ of एव combines after ए sound context, producing ऐ in the combined expression.
ध्यायन्तः + उपासते = ध्यायन्त उपासते
Sandhi name: विसर्गलोपः / विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga is dropped before the following vowel उ.
9F. Samāsa
मत्पराः = तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: अहं परः येषां ते — those for whom I am the supreme goal.
अनन्येन = नञ्-तत्पुरुष sense
Breakdown: न अन्यः — not other / not distracted toward another.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
संन्यस्य is a ल्यबन्त-कृदन्तम् from सम्-नि-अस्, used in the sense “having dedicated.”
ध्यायन्तः is a शतृप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from ध्यै, meaning “meditating.”
उपासते is लट्-लकारः, आत्मनेपदम्, from उप-आस्.
सर्वाणि and कर्माणि are both नपुंसकलिङ्ग-द्वितीया-बहुवचनम्.
योगेन is तृतीया-विभक्ति, showing the means or discipline.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Hanumān Offering Action to Rāma (Rāmāyaṇa)
When Hanumān crossed the ocean to Laṅkā, he was not acting for himself. Every movement of his body, every decision of his mind, and every risk he took belonged to Rāma’s mission.
He searched through Laṅkā with great care. He did not become distracted by the city’s beauty, its wealth, or its danger. His purpose was clear: to find Sītā and serve Bhagavān Rāma.
When he finally saw Sītā in the Aśoka grove, he did not rush impulsively. He waited, observed, chose the right moment, and spoke words that would give her courage.
Later, when he burned Laṅkā and returned, he did not boast of personal achievement. He came back to Rāma with the attitude of a servant who had completed an offering.
This is the spirit of sarvāṇi karmāṇi mayi sannyasya. The action was intense, intelligent, and heroic — but inwardly it was dedicated.
2. Story of Śabarī Waiting for Rāma (Rāmāyaṇa)
Śabarī lived a simple life in the forest, guided by the instruction of her guru. She had no grand position, no scholarship, and no outer power. Her life was made sacred through waiting, service, and devotion.
Every day she cleaned the āśrama. Every day she gathered fruits. Every day she lived with the thought that Rāma would come.
Years passed.
Many people might have grown tired or doubtful. But Śabarī’s mind did not turn elsewhere. Her simple actions — sweeping, gathering, arranging, waiting — were all offerings.
When Rāma finally arrived, she was overwhelmed with joy. She offered fruits with love, carefully choosing what was sweet.
Her life shows that dedication is not limited to dramatic action. Even the smallest daily work becomes sacred when it is offered to Bhagavān with matparatvam — Bhagavān as the center.
3. Story of Sudāmā Visiting Kṛṣṇa (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Sudāmā lived in poverty, but his heart was rich with devotion to Kṛṣṇa. At his wife’s urging, he went to Dvārakā to meet his childhood friend.
He carried with him a small bundle of flattened rice — a poor man’s gift, but given with love.
When he reached Kṛṣṇa’s palace, Kṛṣṇa welcomed him with extraordinary affection. He washed Sudāmā’s feet, seated him with honor, and treated him not as a poor visitor but as one dear to Him.
Sudāmā felt shy about offering the little food he had brought. But Kṛṣṇa took it with delight.
The value was not in the object. The value was in the offering.
Sudāmā did not approach Kṛṣṇa with calculation. He came with devotion and humility. His simple act became meaningful because it was offered to Bhagavān.
This story reminds us that dedication is not measured by external greatness. Even a small action, when offered with the right attitude, becomes sacred.
4. Story of Tyāgarāja and Rāma
Tyāgarāja lived with deep devotion to Rāma. Music was not merely an art for him; it was worship.
Kings and patrons could have offered wealth and recognition. But Tyāgarāja’s heart was not drawn by such rewards. His songs were offerings at the feet of Rāma.
In one famous moment, when royal wealth was offered, he asked through song: “Is wealth greater, or the presence of Rāma?”
For him, the answer was clear.
His action — singing — was not for fame. His talent was not for display. His music became īśvara-arpaṇam, an offering to Bhagavān.
This is a powerful example of matparāḥ. Rāma was not one interest among many. Rāma was the goal, the center, and the meaning of his life.
5. Story of King Ambarīṣa (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
King Ambarīṣa ruled a kingdom, but his mind was fixed on Bhagavān. He did not abandon his duties. He governed, protected, gave, performed worship, and lived responsibly.
But every part of his life was oriented toward Bhagavān.
His mind remembered Bhagavān. His speech praised Bhagavān. His hands served. His ears listened to sacred stories. His feet went to holy places. His actions were not separate from devotion.
Once, the sage Durvāsā became angry with him and created a dangerous situation. Ambarīṣa did not react with pride or fear. He remained humble and steady, trusting Bhagavān.
His life shows that active responsibility and devotion need not be separate. One can live in the world, perform many actions, and still dedicate everything to Bhagavān.
This is the spirit of 12.6 — action continues, but the center changes.
