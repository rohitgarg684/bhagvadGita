1. Shloka
क्लेशोऽधिकतरस्तेषामव्यक्तासक्तचेतसाम् ।
अव्यक्ता हि गतिर्दुःखं देहवद्भिरवाप्यते ॥12.5॥
kleśo'dhikatarasteṣāmavyaktāsaktacetasām ।
avyaktā hi gatirduḥkhaṁ dehavadbhiravāpyate ॥12.5॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that the path of the unmanifest is more difficult for those whose minds are attached to the unmanifest, because the unmanifest goal is attained with difficulty by those with body-identification.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa has just spoken about those who meditate upon nirguṇa brahman. He has also said that such meditators attain Him alone. Now He adds an important qualification: that path involves greater difficulty. The difficulty is not because nirguṇa brahman is inferior, but because the mind that has to contemplate it must be highly prepared.
The word avyakta indicates that the goal is unmanifest. It is not available as an object of sight, hearing, touch, taste, or smell. Since it cannot be held through the senses, the mind does not get the usual support that it gets in form-based meditation. Therefore, when the mind is attached to the unmanifest, the path becomes subtle and demanding.
The main obstacle is indicated by the word dehavadbhiḥ. This refers to those who have strong body-identification or bodily attachment. When we take ourselves primarily as the body, our concerns revolve around physical security, health, comfort, relationships, possessions, and survival. Such a mind naturally thinks in terms of location, form, and limitation.
For a mind strongly centered on the body, meditation on the formless and unobjectifiable reality is difficult. We cannot easily contemplate the unlocalized truth while holding tightly to a localized sense of “I am this body.” Therefore, the stronger the body-identification, the more difficult this path becomes.
This verse is not discouraging nirguṇa contemplation. It is showing the need for preparation. We should understand the difficulty honestly and grow into the required maturity step by step.
4. Story
In the Chāndogya Upaniṣad, there is a powerful teaching story of Indra and Virocana.
Both of them approached Prajāpati with the desire to know the Self. Indra represented the devas, and Virocana represented the asuras. Both were serious enough to stay with the teacher and undergo discipline.
Prajāpati first gave them an instruction connected to the reflection seen in water. When they looked at themselves in the water, they saw the body — decorated, visible, and recognizable.
Virocana was satisfied too quickly. He concluded, “This body itself is the Self.” He returned to the asuras and taught that the body must be beautified, protected, and enjoyed, because the body is the self.
Indra also left at first, but as he reflected, doubts arose. “If the body is blind, does the Self become blind? If the body is injured, is the Self injured? If the body dies, does the Self die?”
He realized that the visible body cannot be the real Self.
So he returned to Prajāpati.
Again and again, Prajāpati led him through subtler levels of understanding. Indra had to outgrow the mistake of identifying the Self with the body, dream, and deep sleep as misunderstood experiences. Only through patient inquiry did he become ready for the subtle teaching.
This story shows exactly why the unmanifest path is difficult. As long as we mistake the body for the Self, we cannot easily understand the formless truth. Virocana stopped at body-identification. Indra continued beyond it.
The path of avyakta gatiḥ requires the maturity to question the body-centered identity.
5. Impact to Current Life
Modern life strengthens body-identification in many ways. We are constantly pushed to think about appearance, health, comfort, success, social image, physical security, and personal control. These concerns are not wrong in themselves, but if they dominate the mind, we become unable to appreciate anything subtler.
A mind absorbed in body-centered concerns naturally wants something visible, emotional, and immediately reassuring. It prefers what can be seen, touched, measured, and possessed. Therefore, formless contemplation may feel dry, abstract, or impossible.
This verse helps us be honest. If our mind is still heavily attached to the body, possessions, and security, we should not pretend that nirguṇa contemplation is easy. We need preparation, maturity, and gradual freedom from excessive dependence on the body.
The teaching is compassionate because it identifies the real obstacle. The issue is not with nirguṇa brahman. The issue is whether the mind is ready to abide in what cannot be objectified.
6. Reflection
How much of my anxiety comes from identifying myself mainly with the body and its security?
Am I trying to reach subtle understanding without preparing the mind properly?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose I ask you to think about a flower. That is easy because you can see its color and shape.
But if I ask you to think about space, that is harder. You cannot hold space or draw it properly.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying that thinking about Bhagavān without any form is difficult, especially when we are used to thinking only about things we can see and touch.”
7B. Story
A child was asked to draw a mango.
That was easy. The child drew its shape, color, and leaf.
Then the teacher said, “Now draw the smell of the mango.”
The child stopped.
“How can I draw smell? I can smell it, but I cannot draw it like a shape.”
The teacher smiled and said, “Some things are real, but they are harder to hold in the mind because they do not have a clear form.”
In the same way, thinking of Bhagavān with a form is easier. Thinking of Bhagavān as formless is much harder.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Is it easier to think about something you can see or something you cannot see?
Can you name something real that is hard to draw?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: avyaktāsaktacetasām = of those whose minds are attached to the unmanifest
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is speaking about those whose minds are committed to the pursuit of nirguṇa brahman. This is the path of the unmanifest.
Step 2: The Difficulty Stated
Phrase: kleśo'dhikataraḥ = the difficulty is greater
The path is not rejected, but its difficulty is clearly stated. Since the unmanifest cannot be held as an ordinary object, the discipline required is greater.
Step 3: The Nature of the Goal
Phrase: avyaktā hi gatiḥ = for the goal is unmanifest
The goal is avyaktā — unmanifest. It is not available to the senses and cannot be objectified like form, sound, touch, taste, or smell.
Step 4: Difficulty of Attainment
Phrase: duḥkhaṁ avāpyate = is attained with difficulty
Because the mind is accustomed to depending on name, form, and object-support, the unmanifest goal is difficult to attain. It demands a subtle and prepared mind.
Step 5: The Main Obstacle
Phrase: dehavadbhiḥ = by those with body-identification
The difficulty is especially for those who are strongly identified with the body. A body-centered mind thinks in terms of location, limitation, protection, pleasure, and fear.
Step 6: The Teaching
Phrase: dehavadbhiravāpyate = it is attained with difficulty by the body-identified
The more we cling to body-identification, the harder it is to appreciate the formless reality. The teaching therefore points to the need for maturity, preparation, and gradual freedom from bodily attachment.

9E. Sandhi
क्लेशः + अधिकतरः = क्लेशोऽधिकतरः
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; अः + अ gives ओऽ with अवग्रहः.
अधिकतरः + तेषाम् = अधिकतरस्तेषाम्
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga changes to स् before the dental consonant त.
तेषाम् + अव्यक्तासक्तचेतसाम् = तेषामव्यक्तासक्तचेतसाम्
Sandhi name: सामान्यव्यञ्जनस्वरसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel अ without special change.
गतिः + दुःखम् = गतिर्दुःखम्
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga after इ changes to र् before the soft consonant द.
देहवद्भिः + अवाप्यते = देहवद्भिरवाप्यते
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga after इ changes to र् before the vowel अ.
9F. Samāsa
अव्यक्तासक्तचेतसाम् = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: अव्यक्ते आसक्तं चेतः येषां ते — those whose minds are attached to the unmanifest.
देहवद्भिः is not a samāsa; it is formed with वतुप्-प्रत्ययः, meaning “those associated with the body” or “those who have body-identification.”
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
अधिकतरः contains तरप्-प्रत्ययः, giving the comparative sense “greater” or “more intense.”
दुःखम् is used adverbially here; this is an अव्ययीभाववत् प्रयोगः in the sense of “with difficulty.”
देहवत् is formed using वतुप्-प्रत्ययः.
अवाप्यते is लट्-लकारः, कर्मणि प्रयोगः, from the धातुः अव-आप्.
अव्यक्ता is a स्त्रीलिङ्ग-विशेषणम् qualifying गतिः.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Bhṛgu and Varuṇa (Taittirīya Upaniṣad)
Bhṛgu once approached his father and teacher, Varuṇa, with a sincere request: “Teach me brahman.”
Varuṇa did not immediately give a long explanation. Instead, he gave Bhṛgu a method of inquiry and asked him to reflect deeply.
Bhṛgu first contemplated food — annam. He saw that all beings are born from food, live by food, and return to food. Since the body is sustained by food, it seemed natural to think that food might be brahman.
But he did not stop there.
He continued his inquiry and saw that life-force, mind, knowledge, and finally ānanda were progressively subtler. Each step required him to move beyond the earlier identification.
This was not a casual movement. At every stage, Bhṛgu had to release a more obvious support and move toward something subtler.
His journey shows why the subtle truth is difficult. We begin with the body and the material world because they are immediate. But brahman is not grasped by stopping at the body. We must refine the mind and move inward step by step.
This is exactly the challenge indicated in 12.5: for one who is strongly body-centered, the unmanifest goal is difficult.
2. Story of Jaḍa Bharata (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Jaḍa Bharata moved through the world in a strange and silent way. To others, he seemed dull and unresponsive. People misunderstood him, mocked him, and treated him harshly.
One day, he was forced to carry the palanquin of King Rahūgaṇa. As he walked, he carefully avoided stepping on tiny creatures on the path. Because of this, the palanquin shook.
The king became angry and insulted him.
But Jaḍa Bharata remained calm.
When he finally spoke, his words were astonishing. He pointed out that the king was identifying with the body and speaking as though one body was carrying another. In truth, the Self is different from the body, and the body alone is involved in such actions.
The king was humbled.
He realized that the one he had dismissed as ignorant was actually established in deep wisdom.
Jaḍa Bharata’s life shows the freedom that comes when body-identification has been loosened. For such a person, honor and insult do not shake the mind in the same way. The unmanifest truth becomes meaningful only when we stop taking the body as the final “I.”
3. Story of Śuka and Vyāsa
Śuka, the son of Vyāsa, was known for extraordinary dispassion from a young age. He did not seem bound by the usual attractions of the world.
When he left home, Vyāsa called out after him with the affection of a father. But Śuka walked on, inwardly free.
There is a traditional account that even when Śuka passed by women bathing, they were not disturbed, because they sensed that his vision was free from body-consciousness. Later, when Vyāsa passed, they felt shy, because Vyāsa still saw distinctions at the level of worldly roles.
The point of the story is not external behavior, but inner vision.
Śuka did not relate to himself or others primarily as bodies. His mind was not caught in the usual body-based divisions.
Such freedom is rare. Most of us see ourselves and others through the body first. That is why the path of the unmanifest is difficult. It asks for a maturity in which the body is no longer the center of identity.
4. Story of Hastāmalaka and Śaṅkarācārya
Once, Śaṅkarācārya met a young boy who appeared outwardly quiet and withdrawn. The boy’s father was concerned because the child did not behave like other children.
Śaṅkarācārya looked at the boy and asked, “Who are you?”
The answer that came was not ordinary.
The boy did not identify himself by name, family, age, body, or role. Instead, he spoke from the standpoint of pure awareness. His understanding was so clear that it was as though the truth rested in his hand like an āmalaka fruit.
He came to be known as Hastāmalaka.
This story shows a mind that was not confused by body-identification. He did not say, “I am this body” or “I am this social identity.” His clarity was already turned toward the Self.
For most people, such clarity is difficult because the body-sense is powerful. That is why 12.5 warns that the unmanifest goal is attained with difficulty by those attached to bodily identity.
5. Story of the Rope and the Snake (Traditional Vedānta Teaching)
A traveler once walked at dusk along a village path. In the fading light, he suddenly saw what appeared to be a snake lying ahead.
Fear arose instantly. His body trembled. His heart beat faster. He stepped back, imagining danger.
Then someone brought a lamp.
In the light, the “snake” was seen to be only a rope.
Nothing had happened to the rope. The fear came from misunderstanding.
In the same way, when we misunderstand ourselves as merely the body, fear naturally arises — fear of loss, fear of pain, fear of death, fear of insecurity.
The truth is not newly created. It has to be recognized.
But as long as the mistake is strong, the mind reacts as though the body-centered identity is absolutely real. That is why the path to the unmanifest requires preparation. We need the light of understanding to correct the basic error.
