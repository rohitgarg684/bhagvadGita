1. Shloka
अथैतदप्यशक्तोऽसि कर्तुं मद्योगमाश्रितः ।
सर्वकर्मफलत्यागं ततः कुरु यतात्मवान् ॥12.11॥
athaitadapyaśakto'si kartuṁ madyogamāśritaḥ ।
sarvakarmaphalatyāgaṁ tataḥ kuru yatātmavān ॥12.11॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says: if we are unable to do even this, then, taking refuge in His worship, we should renounce the results of all actions with self-mastery.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now gives the lowest and most accessible step in the ladder of bhakti-yoga. If we are not ready for niṣkāma karma, if we are not ready for abhyāsa-yoga, if we are not ready for Viśvarūpa upāsanā, and if we are certainly not ready for nirguṇa jñāna-yoga, then we should begin here.
This step is meant for a person who still has many personal desires. Such a person may be concerned with family, children, health, education, house, money, success, security, and many other legitimate worldly goals. The teaching does not ask such a person to suppress all desires immediately. Suppressed desires will continue to disturb the mind. Therefore, the instruction is: fulfill legitimate desires through dhārmic means, but change the attitude toward the result.
The key instruction is sarvakarmaphalatyāgam kuru — renounce the results of all actions. This does not mean physically throwing away the result. It means mentally offering the result to Bhagavān first, and then receiving it back as Bhagavān’s prasāda. If we build a house, we dedicate it to Bhagavān and live in it as Bhagavān’s gift. If we receive food, clothing, success, or family joy, we first recognize it as prasāda.
Even sakāma karma can become karma-yoga when the result is received as Bhagavān’s prasāda. Ordinary selfish action does not purify because selfishness itself is impurity. But when the result is offered to Bhagavān and received with prasāda-buddhi, even such action slowly purifies the mind. From this first step, we gradually grow into niṣkāma karma, then upāsanā, and eventually jñāna-yoga.
4. Story
In the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, the Govardhana episode beautifully shows how an ordinary action can be transformed through offering.
In Vṛndāvana, the people were preparing for the annual worship of Indra. They depended on rain, cattle, grass, and land, and they believed that Indra’s grace gave them prosperity. Food was prepared, offerings were gathered, and the community was ready for ritual worship.
Then young Kṛṣṇa questioned the elders.
He asked them to think more deeply about their life. Their cows grazed on Govardhana. The mountain gave grass, streams, caves, trees, and shelter. Their daily life was sustained by the land, the cows, and the whole order around them.
Kṛṣṇa guided them to offer their gratitude to Govardhana instead. The people prepared heaps of food, decorated the cows, gathered together, and offered everything with reverence. What had begun as a customary ritual became a conscious offering to Bhagavān manifest through the very world that supported them.
After the offering, the people partook of the food as prasāda.
This is the heart of sarvakarmaphalatyāga. The food was not wasted. The people still ate. But before enjoying, they offered. By offering first and receiving later, the attitude changed.
The action remained part of life, but the result was no longer treated as “mine alone.” It became Bhagavān’s prasāda.
5. Impact to Current Life
This verse is extremely practical for modern life. Many of us are not yet ready to give up personal goals. We want a good education, a home, career growth, financial stability, children’s success, family health, and social security. This verse does not ask us to pretend that we have no such desires.
Instead, it gives a realistic spiritual beginning. Pursue legitimate goals through dhārmic means, but do not hold the results with egoistic ownership. Before enjoying any result, inwardly offer it to Bhagavān. Then receive it as prasāda.
This attitude reduces arrogance when things go well and bitterness when things do not go as expected. It helps us see life not merely as personal achievement, but as participation in Bhagavān’s order.
Even small actions can become spiritual practice. Eating food, entering a new home, wearing new clothes, receiving salary, celebrating success, or facing failure — all can be connected to Bhagavān through prasāda-buddhi.
6. Reflection
Do I treat my achievements as entirely mine, or can I offer them to Bhagavān first?
Can I receive both success and difficulty as prasāda, without arrogance or bitterness?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose you get your favorite sweet. You may want to eat it immediately.
But before eating, you can place it before Bhagavān and say, ‘Thank you.’ Then you eat it as prasāda.
The sweet is still yours to enjoy, but now your heart becomes thankful. Kṛṣṇa is teaching that whatever good result we get, we can first offer it to Bhagavān and then enjoy it with gratitude.”
7B. Story
A child got a new box of crayons.
The child was very excited and wanted to start coloring immediately.
The parent said, “Before using them, let us place them near Bhagavān and say thank you.”
The child asked, “Will Bhagavān take the crayons?”
The parent smiled and said, “No. We are offering our thanks. Then you can use them happily as prasāda.”
The child placed the crayons near Bhagavān, folded hands, and then began drawing.
The crayons were the same, but the child’s heart had changed. Now the drawing felt like a gift.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What is one thing you can say “thank you” to Bhagavān for today?
Can you offer something before enjoying it?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: The Next Descent
Phrase: atha etad api aśakto'si kartum = if you are unable to do even this
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now addresses one who is not able to practice even the previous step. This is a compassionate descent to the most accessible level of karma-yoga.
Step 2: Taking Refuge in Worship
Phrase: madyogamāśritaḥ = taking refuge in My yoga / My worship
We should still remain connected to Bhagavān. Even if the life is filled with personal desires and worldly duties, it should not be cut off from Bhagavān. This life too can become a form of bhakti-yoga when Bhagavān is included.
Step 3: Renouncing the Results
Phrase: sarvakarmaphalatyāgaṁ kuru = renounce the results of all actions
This does not mean throwing away the results. It means offering all karma-phala to Bhagavān and then receiving it back as prasāda. The result may still be used, enjoyed, or shared, but the attitude of ownership changes.
Step 4: All Kinds of Results
Phrase: sarvakarmaphala = the results of all actions
This includes the results of kāmya karmas, ordinary worldly actions, and even unavoidable mixed actions in daily life. Before enjoying anything, we dedicate it to Bhagavān.
Step 5: With Self-Mastery
Phrase: yatātmavān = one with self-restraint / self-mastery
Even at this entry level, some discipline is required. Desires may be fulfilled, but only through legitimate and dhārmic means. The mind should learn gratitude, restraint, and prasāda-buddhi.
Step 6: The Teaching
Phrase: tataḥ kuru = then do
The teaching is: begin where we are. If higher practices are not possible, do not abandon spiritual life. Perform legitimate actions, offer their results to Bhagavān, receive them as prasāda, and gradually allow the mind to become purer.

	9D. Anvayaḥ
अथ एतत् अपि कर्तुम् अशक्तः असि, मद्योगम् आश्रितः ततः यतात्मवान् सर्वकर्मफलत्यागम् कुरु ।
9E. Sandhi
अथ + एतत् = अथैतत्
Sandhi name: वृद्धिसन्धिः; final अ combines with initial ए to form ऐ.
एतत् + अपि = एतदपि
Sandhi name: व्यञ्जनसन्धिः / जश्त्वसन्धिः; final त् becomes द् before the following vowel.
अपि + अशक्तः = अप्यशक्तः
Sandhi name: यण् सन्धिः; final इ becomes य् before the following vowel अ.
अशक्तः + असि = अशक्तोऽसि
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final अः becomes ओ before अ, with अवग्रहः marking the elided अ.
कर्तुम् + मद्योगम् = कर्तुं मद्योगम्
Sandhi name: अनुस्वारादेशः; final म् is represented as अनुस्वारः before the following consonant म.
मद्योगम् + आश्रितः = मद्योगमाश्रितः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel आ without special change.
सर्वकर्मफलत्यागम् + ततः = सर्वकर्मफलत्यागं ततः
Sandhi name: अनुस्वारादेशः; final म् is represented as अनुस्वारः before the following consonant त.
ततः + कुरु = ततः कुरु
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; visarga remains before the hard consonant क.
9F. Samāsa
मद्योगम् = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: मम योगः — My yoga / My worship.
सर्वकर्मफलत्यागम् = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: सर्वेषां कर्मणां फलानां त्यागः — renunciation of the fruits of all actions.
यतात्मवान् = बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: यतः आत्मा यस्य सः — one whose self/mind is restrained.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
कर्तुम् is a तुमुनन्त-कृदन्तम् from कृ धातुः, meaning “to do.”
आश्रितः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from आ-श्रि धातुः, meaning “having taken refuge in.”
अशक्तः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त form with negative prefix अ-, meaning “unable.”
कुरु is लोट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम् from कृ धातुः, used as an instruction.
यतात्मवान् carries the sense of self-restraint and discipline.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Govardhana Pūjā (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
In Vṛndāvana, people lived close to the land, cows, forests, rain, and mountains. Their prosperity came through a web of visible and invisible blessings.
When the time came for worship, Kṛṣṇa redirected their attention to Govardhana. The mountain nourished their cows, gave grass, water, shade, fruits, and shelter. Their life was not separate from this sacred order.
The people prepared great quantities of food and offered everything with reverence.
After the offering, they did not discard the food. They received it back as prasāda.
This is a beautiful model of karma-phala-tyāga. We work, prepare, earn, cook, and receive. But before enjoying, we offer. Then the same result becomes sacred.
2. Story of Mahābali and Vāmana (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Mahābali was a powerful king who had gained great prosperity through effort, courage, and sacrifice. When Vāmana, the dwarf brāhmaṇa, came to him and asked for three steps of land, Bali agreed.
Śukrācārya warned him that this was no ordinary brāhmaṇa. This was Bhagavān Himself.
Bali understood the danger. Giving the promise might cost him everything. Yet he did not withdraw.
Vāmana grew into Trivikrama and covered the worlds in two steps. For the third, Bali offered his own head.
This was no longer ordinary giving. Bali released ownership over everything he had gained — kingdom, power, wealth, and finally himself.
His story shows the deeper direction of karma-phala-tyāga. What we call “mine” is gradually recognized as belonging to Bhagavān.
3. Story of the Fruit Seller and Kṛṣṇa (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
One day in Vṛndāvana, a fruit seller came calling through the streets. Little Kṛṣṇa heard her and came running with tiny hands full of grain to exchange for fruit.
But as He ran, most of the grains slipped through His fingers and fell to the ground.
When He reached her, only a few grains remained.
The fruit seller saw the child’s face and her heart melted. She filled His hands with fruits anyway.
When she later looked into her basket, it was filled with jewels.
The outer exchange was small. But the inner offering was complete.
This story shows that Bhagavān does not measure the material value of what is offered. When the heart offers, the result becomes prasāda.
4. Story of Purandara Dāsa
Purandara Dāsa was once known as Śrīnivāsa Nāyaka, a wealthy but miserly man. His life was centered on possession and calculation.
Through a divine incident, his heart was shaken open. He saw the emptiness of hoarding and the higher value of devotion.
He gave up his pride in wealth and dedicated his life to Bhagavān.
From then on, his songs became offerings. His talent, time, voice, and life were no longer held as personal property. They became service to Bhagavān.
His transformation shows how the attitude toward wealth and results can change. What was once “mine” becomes an offering.
5. Story of Janābāī and Viṭṭhala
Janābāī lived a simple life of service. Her daily work included grinding grain, cleaning, and performing household tasks.
But inwardly, she did not see these as ordinary burdens. She remembered Viṭṭhala while working.
In the stories of her devotion, Viṭṭhala Himself is said to have joined her in daily chores, helping her grind grain and complete household work.
The meaning is beautiful: when work is connected to Bhagavān, even simple labor becomes worship.
Janābāī did not need a grand ritual to offer her life. She offered the result of daily work through remembrance and devotion.
Her life shows that karma-phala-tyāga can begin in the kitchen, courtyard, workplace, and every ordinary corner of life.
