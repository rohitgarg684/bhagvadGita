1. Shloka
अर्जुन उवाच
एवं सततयुक्ता ये भक्तास्त्वां पर्युपासते ।
ये चाप्यक्षरमव्यक्तं तेषां के योगवित्तमाः ॥12.1॥
arjuna uvāca
evaṁ satatayuktā ye bhaktāstvāṁ paryupāsate ।
ye cāpyakṣaramavyaktaṁ teṣāṁ ke yogavittamāḥ ॥12.1॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Arjuna asks: among the ever-steadfast devotees who meditate upon Bhagavān as taught before, and those who meditate upon the imperishable unmanifest brahman, who are the best yogis?
3. Meaning
The chapter opens with a question from Arjuna based on the earlier teaching. This is not an independent question, but a question that arises from what has already been taught. In the previous chapter, Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa revealed Viśvarūpa īśvara, and Arjuna now wants clarity regarding the relative place of saguṇa and nirguṇa contemplation.
In the first line, Arjuna describes the saguṇa upāsakas. evaṁ means “as taught before,” that is, as presented in the previous chapter. Here the reference is to Viśvarūpa saguṇa īśvara — Bhagavān as the entire cosmos. These devotees are satatayuktāḥ: they meditate with constant commitment, sincerely and steadfastly.
Then Arjuna introduces the second group: those who meditate upon akṣaram avyaktaṁ. akṣaram is nirguṇa brahman, the attributeless absolute. Since it is nirguṇa, it is avyaktam — not available to any sense organ, not objectifiable through seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, or tasting. Such meditation is therefore of a very different order.
Arjuna’s question is straightforward: between these two groups — the saguṇa meditators and the nirguṇa meditators — who are superior? He is asking about the meditators, and indirectly about the relative place of these two orientations.
4. Story
In the Mahābhārata, there is a quiet but powerful contrast between two great souls: Bhīṣma and Kuntī.
Bhīṣma was a man of extraordinary discipline, clarity, and subtle understanding. Even while lying on the bed of arrows, with the body in great pain, his mind remained collected and luminous. He spoke of dharma with precision, reflected deeply, and remained inwardly steady. His life shows the grandeur of a mind that can abide in truth with detachment and depth.
Kuntī, on the other hand, related to Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa in a very different way. Her heart moved toward Him with surrender, intimacy, and devotion. In her prayers, she did not speak as a philosopher analyzing reality. She spoke as one who had discovered refuge in Bhagavān. She had seen suffering, uncertainty, exile, and danger — and through all of it, her heart turned again and again to Kṛṣṇa.
Both stood before the same Bhagavān.
One approached through subtle understanding and inner steadiness. The other approached through devotion, surrender, and loving remembrance.
Neither was trivial. Neither was superficial.
Seeing the presence of these two orientations in life itself, Arjuna’s question becomes natural: when there are devotees who worship Bhagavān in a personal way, and others who turn toward the imperishable and unmanifest, who among them is superior in Yoga?
That is the spirit of the question in this verse.


5. Impact to Current Life
We often face the same kind of confusion Arjuna is facing here. Today too, we encounter many valid approaches — devotion, meditation, inquiry, study, ritual, service — and we begin to compare them instead of understanding how they fit into spiritual growth.
This verse shows that confusion is not a weakness. A sincere person will naturally want clarity. But it also suggests that comparison is not always simple. We may compare two paths as though they are alternatives, when in fact they may belong to different stages or functions in inner growth.
In modern life, this appears in other forms too. We compare methods of learning, compare different spiritual teachers, compare styles of practice, and end up restless. The real need is not endless comparison, but clarity about what each path is and what role it plays.
6. Reflection
When I see different valid paths, do I become restless and comparative, or do I seek clear understanding?
Am I looking only for “the best option,” or am I trying to understand the place of each approach in growth?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose two people want to learn the same thing, but one likes learning through pictures and another likes learning through quiet thinking.
Then we may ask: which way is better?
Arjuna is asking Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa something like that. Some people pray to Bhagavān in a form they can relate to. Some people think about Bhagavān as something invisible and beyond form. Arjuna wants to know which of these is better.”
7B. Story
A child wanted to learn music.
One teacher said, “First sing the song and feel it with your heart.”
Another teacher said, “First understand the notes and patterns carefully.”
The child became confused. “Should I sing with feeling, or should I learn the notes?”
The parent smiled and said, “Both help, but you must first understand what each one is doing.”
The child relaxed. The question was not silly. It was an important question.
In the same way, Arjuna is asking Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa to explain two kinds of spiritual practice.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Have you ever been confused when two people teach you differently?
What do you do when you want to understand which way is right?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Context
Phrase: arjuna uvāca = Arjuna said
Arjuna begins with a question that arises from what has already been taught. This is a follow-up question based on the previous chapter’s teaching.
Step 2: First Group Introduced
Phrase: evaṁ satatayuktā ye bhaktāstvāṁ paryupāsate = those ever-steadfast devotees who meditate upon You as taught before
evaṁ means “as described before.” The reference is to Viśvarūpa saguṇa īśvara presented in the eleventh chapter. These devotees meditate with satatayuktatā — constant commitment and steadfastness.
Step 3: Second Group Introduced
Phrase: ye cāpyakṣaramavyaktaṁ = and those who meditate upon the imperishable unmanifest
Here Arjuna refers to another group who meditate upon akṣaram — nirguṇa brahman. Since it is nirguṇa, it is avyaktam — not perceptible to any sense organ and not available as an object.
Step 4: The Nature of Nirguṇa Meditation
Phrase: akṣaramavyaktaṁ = the imperishable, unmanifest
Since nirguṇa brahman cannot be objectified, meditation upon it is unlike ordinary meditation on an object. The only way such brahman can be contemplated is as the very subject, the meditator himself or herself. Thus nirguṇa brahma upāsanā becomes ātma-dhyānam.
Step 5: The Question Proper
Phrase: teṣāṁ ke yogavittamāḥ = among them, who are the best knowers of Yoga
Arjuna asks: between these two groups, who is superior? The question is about the meditators, and indirectly about the relative place of saguṇa and nirguṇa contemplation.

ये + च = येच

चापि + अक्षरम् = चाप्यक्षरम्

9F. Samāsa
सततयुक्ताः = कर्मधारय समास
योगवित्तमाः = तद्धित-प्रयुक्त superlative form
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
पर्युपासते is an आत्मनेपदी present-tense verb.
In the second half, the verb is understood from the first half and is to be supplied elliptically.
अव्यक्तम् qualifies अक्षरम्.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Nārada (Bhāgavatam)
As a young boy, Nārada had the blessing of serving great sages during the rainy season. He moved quietly among them, offering simple service, listening to their conversations, and absorbing the atmosphere of their lives.
He was not yet learned. He did not know philosophy. But he listened with reverence.
The sages spoke of Bhagavān, sang of Bhagavān, and lived with a kind of inward fullness that left a deep mark on the boy. Slowly, something awakened in him. What began as admiration became attraction. What began as attraction became devotion.
After the sages left, Nārada carried that remembrance within him. In solitude, he sat in meditation and turned his mind wholly toward Bhagavān.
For a brief moment, he had a direct vision of the divine presence.
It was enough to transform his life.
Though that vision disappeared, his direction became irreversible. He did not waste his life comparing paths endlessly. His heart had found its center.
This story shows the path of loving, steady devotion to Bhagavān — the kind of path Arjuna sees and places in one side of his question.
2. Story of Sanatkumāra and Nārada (Chāndogya Upaniṣad)
There is a beautiful Upaniṣadic dialogue between Nārada and Sanatkumāra.
Nārada approached the sage not as an ignorant man, but as one already filled with knowledge. He had studied many branches of learning — scriptures, arts, sciences, and sacred disciplines. Yet inwardly he was still not at peace.
So he came and said, in effect, “I know many things, but I do not know that by which sorrow comes to an end.”
Sanatkumāra then began leading him step by step from the limited to the limitless.
He did not begin with emotional consolation. Nor did he merely praise devotion in form. He unfolded a subtler vision of reality — a movement from names and finite objects toward what is vast, boundaryless, and beyond narrow limitation.
Nārada, who had already known devotion, now stood before a deeper inquiry.
This story helps us understand why Arjuna’s question is not superficial. Spiritual life includes both devotion to Bhagavān in accessible form and inquiry into the limitless reality beyond limiting conditions.
3. Story of Hanumān (Rāmāyaṇa)
When Hanumān stood on the seashore and looked toward Laṅkā, the mission before him was enormous. The ocean was vast, the destination far away, and the task filled with danger.
Many vānaras around him began estimating their strength. Some spoke confidently, some hesitated, some admitted their limits.
For a moment, Hanumān too stood still.
Then Jāmbavān reminded him of who he truly was and what strength lay hidden within him.
But Hanumān’s real power did not come merely from physical might. As his mind turned toward Rāma, all confusion disappeared. He remembered Bhagavān with total absorption — His form, His purpose, His trust.
That remembrance gathered his mind, removed hesitation, and released strength.
Then came the leap.
Through wind, sky, danger, and opposition, Hanumān’s mind never lost its anchor in Bhagavān.
This story stands on the side of saguṇa upāsanā — the power of devotion to Bhagavān in a personal form, with the mind fixed steadily upon Him.
4. Story of Yājñavalkya and Maitreyī (Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad)
When Yājñavalkya prepared to leave household life, he spoke to Maitreyī about dividing his wealth.
But Maitreyī was not interested in comfort or possession alone. She asked a profound question: “Can wealth give immortality?”
Yājñavalkya answered plainly: no.
That answer opened the door to a deeper teaching. Maitreyī then asked for that knowledge by which immortality may be known.
Yājñavalkya led her away from dependence on finite things and toward the truth of the Self — not as one more object in the world, but as the very reality because of which all relationships, experiences, and values are meaningful.
This teaching does not move through a visible divine form. It moves through subtle inquiry into the imperishable reality.
This story stands on the other side of Arjuna’s question — the side of akṣaram avyaktaṁ.
5. Story of Dhruva (Bhāgavatam)
Dhruva was still a child when hurt and rejection entered his heart. He had gone to his father with a simple longing, only to be turned away.
Wounded and confused, he went to his mother. She did not inflame his pain. Instead, she directed him toward Bhagavān.
“If you want something that cannot be taken away, seek Bhagavān.”
Those words entered deeply into Dhruva’s heart.
He left home and went into the forest. Under the guidance of Nārada, his mind slowly became disciplined. What began in pain matured into one-pointed devotion.
He meditated with increasing steadiness until his whole mind rested in Bhagavān.
In time, Bhagavān appeared before him.
Dhruva’s story is not merely about devotion producing a result. It is about the mind gaining steadiness through sustained turning toward Bhagavān.
That too helps us understand why Arjuna asks his question in the first place: what is the place of such devotion, and how does it compare with meditation on the imperishable?
