1. Shloka
तेषामहं समुद्धर्ता मृत्युसंसारसागरात् ।
भवामि नचिरात्पार्थ मय्यावेशितचेतसाम् ॥12.7॥
teṣāmahaṁ samuddhartā mṛtyusaṁsārasāgarāt ।
bhavāmi nacirātpārtha mayyāveśitacetasām ॥12.7॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that for those whose minds are fixed on Him, He soon becomes the rescuer from the ocean of saṁsāra, which is marked by mortality.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is continuing the teaching of the previous verse. These devotees are practicing Viśvarūpa upāsanā. They dedicate all actions to Bhagavān, keep Bhagavān as the primary goal, meditate upon Him with undivided attention, and keep their minds set on Him.
Such devotees do not look upon Bhagavān merely as a means for material ends. In ordinary sakāma bhakti, Bhagavān may be treated as a means to obtain wealth, health, success, protection, or comfort. But here, Bhagavān is not merely the means; Bhagavān is the end. This is niṣkāma bhakti, the devotion of one who wants Bhagavān Himself.
For such devotees, Bhagavān says, ahaṁ samuddhartā bhavāmi — I become the rescuer. The rescue is from mṛtyu-saṁsāra-sāgara, the ocean of saṁsāra, which is filled with the problem of mortality. Death is the central fear because whatever is near and dear has to be left behind.
Bhagavān also says na cirāt — before long. This does not mean that mokṣa is given immediately from the fourth rung itself. Mokṣa requires jñānam, and jñānam requires jñāna-yogyatā. Therefore, Bhagavān rescues by making the devotee fit for the next step. He provides the necessary conditions, qualifications, guidance, and guru. If needed, Bhagavān Himself becomes the guru, just as He becomes the guru for Arjuna.
4. Story
In the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, there is the moving story of Gajendra, the elephant king.
Gajendra lived in strength and majesty. He roamed the forest with his herd and ruled among elephants with pride and power. One day, he entered a beautiful lake filled with lotuses. Surrounded by his family, he played in the water, enjoying the coolness and beauty around him.
Suddenly, a crocodile seized his leg.
At first, Gajendra fought with all his strength. He pulled, struggled, and tried to free himself. His family watched helplessly. The battle continued for a long time. The elephant was powerful on land, but the crocodile was stronger in water. Slowly, Gajendra’s strength began to fail.
In that helpless moment, his pride broke. He realized that his body, strength, family, and power could not save him.
Then, from deep within, he turned to Bhagavān.
He lifted a lotus with his trunk and prayed with total surrender. His mind was no longer scattered. It was set on Bhagavān alone.
Bhagavān Viṣṇu came swiftly and rescued him from the crocodile.
This story beautifully reflects the promise of 12.7. When the mind is truly turned toward Bhagavān, Bhagavān becomes samuddhartā — the rescuer. The crocodile represents saṁsāra, the lake represents the field of worldly enjoyment, and Gajendra’s helplessness represents the moment we discover that body-strength and worldly supports are not enough.
5. Impact to Current Life
We often try to handle life entirely through personal control. We depend on our intelligence, planning, money, health, relationships, and social support. These are useful, but they are limited.
When life becomes overwhelming, we discover that not everything is under our control. Illness, loss, aging, uncertainty, death, and emotional suffering reveal the limits of our personal strength.
This verse does not teach passivity. It teaches a shift in inner dependence. We still act, think, choose, and work. But we gradually learn not to carry the entire burden alone. We place the mind on Bhagavān and allow spiritual trust to enter our life.
The saṁsāra ocean is not merely outside. It is the repeated cycle of anxiety, fear, attachment, insecurity, and mortality. When the mind is set on Bhagavān as the goal, life gains direction, and Bhagavān’s grace appears as inner maturity, guidance, right conditions, right teachers, and eventual freedom.
6. Reflection
Do I look upon Bhagavān mainly as a means for solving worldly problems, or as the goal of life itself?
When life feels like an ocean, do I depend only on my own strength, or do I turn my mind toward Bhagavān?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Imagine someone is swimming in a big ocean and gets tired. If a strong boat comes and rescues them, they feel safe.
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa is saying that life can sometimes feel like a big ocean. We may feel worried, tired, or afraid. But if we keep our mind on Bhagavān and trust Him, He helps us cross.”
7B. Story
A child was learning to swim.
At first, the child held the edge of the pool tightly. Then the teacher said, “I am right here. Try to float.”
The child was scared and said, “What if I sink?”
The teacher smiled and said, “I will not let you sink.”
Slowly, the child trusted the teacher and tried. Whenever the child became afraid, the teacher held and guided the child.
After some time, the child became more confident.
In the same way, Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that when we keep our mind on Him, He helps us cross the difficult ocean of life.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
When you feel scared, who helps you feel safe?
Can you remember Bhagavān when you feel worried?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: The Devotees Being Referred To
Phrase: teṣām = for them
This refers to the devotees described in the previous verse — those who dedicate all actions to Bhagavān, keep Him as the primary goal, meditate upon Him with undivided attention, and keep the mind absorbed in Him.
Step 2: Mind Set on Bhagavān
Phrase: mayyāveśitacetasām = whose minds are set on Me
These devotees do not treat Bhagavān merely as a means for worldly gain. Their mind is absorbed in Bhagavān as the goal. This is the attitude of niṣkāma bhakti, where Bhagavān is both the means and the end.
Step 3: Bhagavān’s Role
Phrase: ahaṁ samuddhartā bhavāmi = I become the rescuer
Bhagavān takes the role of rescuer for such devotees. This does not mean bypassing the required spiritual growth. It means Bhagavān provides what is needed for their onward movement — qualification, circumstances, guidance, and guru.
Step 4: What We Are Rescued From
Phrase: mṛtyusaṁsārasāgarāt = from the ocean of saṁsāra marked by death
Saṁsāra is compared to an ocean because it is vast, difficult to cross, and filled with danger. The most frightening feature of saṁsāra is mṛtyu, death, because everything connected with the body has to be left behind.
Step 5: The Timeframe
Phrase: na cirāt = before long
Bhagavān says the rescue will not be endlessly delayed. But the meaning is not that mokṣa is given without jñānam. The devotee is soon led toward the necessary next step — fitness for jñāna-yoga.
Step 6: Personal Assurance
Phrase: pārtha = O Arjuna
By addressing Arjuna directly, Bhagavān makes the teaching intimate and reassuring. The devotee who has placed the mind on Bhagavān is not abandoned in the ocean of saṁsāra.

9E. Sandhi
तेषाम् + अहम् = तेषामहम्
Sandhi name: सामान्यव्यञ्जनस्वरसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel अ without special change.
तेषामहम् + समुद्धर्ता = तेषामहं समुद्धर्ता
Sandhi name: अनुस्वारादेशः; final म् is represented as अनुस्वारः before the following consonant स.
न + चिरात् = नचिरात्
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; the words join without phonetic change.
नचिरात् + पार्थ = नचिरात्पार्थ
Sandhi name: व्यञ्जनसन्धिः; final त् joins with प् without change.
मयि + आवेशितचेतसाम् = मय्यावेशितचेतसाम्
Sandhi name: यण् सन्धिः; final इ of मयि becomes य् before the following vowel आ.
9F. Samāsa
मृत्युसंसारसागरात् = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष / कर्मधारय-तत्पुरुष sense
Breakdown: मृत्युः एव विशेषणरूपेण संसारः, स एव सागरः — the ocean of saṁsāra marked by death.
Practical sense: from the ocean of mortal saṁsāra.
आवेशितचेतसाम् = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: मयि आवेशितं चेतः येषां ते — those whose minds are fixed in Me.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
समुद्धर्ता is a तृच्-प्रत्ययान्त-कर्तृवाचक-नामपदम् from सम्-उद्-धृ, meaning “rescuer” or “one who lifts up.”
भवामि is लट्-लकारः, उत्तमपुरुषः, एकवचनम्, परस्मैपदम् from भू धातुः.
आवेशित is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from आ-विश्.
चिरात् is used with न to form the expression न चिरात्, meaning “not after a long time,” i.e., “soon.”
पार्थ is सम्बोधनप्रथमा, directly addressing Arjuna.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Gajendra Mokṣa (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Gajendra, the elephant king, lived with strength, pride, and majesty. Surrounded by his herd, he entered a beautiful lake one day and played among the lotuses.
But suddenly, a crocodile caught his leg.
At first Gajendra fought with all his power. The lake shook, the herd panicked, and the struggle continued. But the crocodile was in its own element, and slowly Gajendra’s strength began to fail.
His family could not save him. His body could not save him. His pride could not save him.
In that helplessness, something deep awakened. Gajendra turned fully to Bhagavān. Lifting a lotus with his trunk, he prayed with total surrender.
Bhagavān Viṣṇu came swiftly and rescued him.
This is the promise of samuddhartā. When worldly strength fails and the mind turns wholly to Bhagavān, Bhagavān becomes the rescuer.
2. Story of Draupadī’s Surrender (Mahābhārata)
In the Kaurava court, Draupadī was dragged into a situation of unbearable humiliation. She first tried to reason. She questioned dharma. She appealed to the elders. She looked for protection among those who should have protected her.
But one by one, all visible supports failed.
Finally, in total helplessness, she lifted both hands and called out to Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa.
At that moment, her dependence shifted completely. She was no longer holding on to human support alone. Her mind turned fully to Bhagavān.
Bhagavān protected her.
This story shows that rescue does not always come when ego is strong. It comes when the mind truly turns to Bhagavān and recognizes Him as the ultimate refuge.
3. Story of Vibhīṣaṇa’s Śaraṇāgati (Rāmāyaṇa)
Vibhīṣaṇa lived in Laṅkā among the rākṣasas, but his heart was aligned with dharma. He advised Rāvaṇa to return Sītā and avoid destruction, but Rāvaṇa rejected him harshly.
At last, Vibhīṣaṇa left Laṅkā and came to Bhagavān Rāma.
The vānaras were suspicious. “He is Rāvaṇa’s brother. Can we trust him?”
But Rāma’s response was clear. One who comes seeking refuge must be protected.
Vibhīṣaṇa came not for wealth or power, but for dharma and refuge in Bhagavān. Rāma accepted him.
This story shows Bhagavān as protector and rescuer. When the mind turns from adharma toward Bhagavān, grace opens the next step.
4. Story of Mārkaṇḍeya (Purāṇic Tradition)
Mārkaṇḍeya was destined to die at a young age. But from childhood, his mind was devoted to Bhagavān Śiva.
When the appointed time came, Yama approached to take him. Mārkaṇḍeya clung to the liṅga and turned completely to Bhagavān.
In that moment, Bhagavān Śiva appeared and protected him from death.
The story powerfully reflects the phrase mṛtyu-saṁsāra-sāgara. Death is the great fear in saṁsāra, but the one whose mind is absorbed in Bhagavān discovers a refuge deeper than mortality.
5. Story of Ajāmila (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Ajāmila had lived a fallen life, far from the discipline he once knew. At the end of his life, fear rose as death approached.
In that final moment, he called out the name “Nārāyaṇa,” though he was calling his son.
Yet the name of Bhagavān carried power.
The messengers of Yama could not take him, and a new opening came into his life. He was given the chance to turn back toward Bhagavān consciously and sincerely.
Ajāmila’s story shows that Bhagavān’s grace can reach even one who has fallen, when the connection to Bhagavān is awakened. The rescue from saṁsāra begins when the mind turns toward Bhagavān.
