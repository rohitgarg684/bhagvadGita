1. Shloka
अथ चित्तं समाधातुं न शक्नोषि मयि स्थिरम् ।
अभ्यासयोगेन ततो मामिच्छाप्तुं धनञ्जय ॥12.9॥
atha cittaṁ samādhātuṁ na śaknoṣi mayi sthiram ।
abhyāsayogena tato māmicchāptuṁ dhanañjaya ॥12.9॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says: if we are not able to steadily fix the mind on Viśvarūpa īśvara, then we should seek to reach Him through abhyāsa-yoga, the repeated practice of iṣṭa-devatā upāsanā.
3. Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now comes one step lower in the ladder. In the previous stage, the instruction was Viśvarūpa upāsanā — seeing the entire universe as Bhagavān and fixing the mind and intellect on Him. But this requires an expanded mind. If the mind is narrow because of strong rāga-dveṣa, it cannot easily appreciate the ocean, sky, stars, trees, mountains, and the whole universe as Bhagavān.
When strong attachments and aversions dominate, the mind keeps circling around only a few objects — what it likes and what it dislikes. Such a mind cannot easily expand into Viśvarūpa contemplation. Therefore, Bhagavān gives a more accessible step: abhyāsa-yoga.
Here abhyāsa-yoga means iṣṭa-devatā upāsanā — meditation upon a chosen form of Bhagavān. Instead of trying to embrace the entire universe as Bhagavān, we begin with one form that the mind can love, visualize, and return to repeatedly. That form may be a personal deity such as Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, Śiva, Devī, Gaṇeśa, or any iṣṭa-devatā. It may also be a symbol used for meditation, such as Sūrya or Śrīcakra.
This upāsanā is not merely physical pūjā. Upāsanā is primarily a mental discipline. We withdraw the senses, turn the mind inward, and visualize Bhagavān in the heart. Through repeated practice, the extrovert mind slowly becomes inward-looking and capable of steadiness.
So the teaching is compassionate and practical. If Viśvarūpa meditation is too difficult, we need not give up. We can begin with a chosen form or symbol, practice regularly, and gradually prepare the mind for a larger vision.
4. Story
In the life of Āṇḍāḷ, we see the power of a mind that grows through loving repetition of one chosen form of Bhagavān.
Āṇḍāḷ grew up in Śrīvilliputtūr, in the home of Periyāḻvār, who served Bhagavān Viṣṇu with devotion. From childhood, her heart was drawn to Bhagavān Raṅganātha. For her, He was not a distant idea. He was the one she loved, remembered, sang to, and longed for.
Every day, she lived in that remembrance. She sang verses filled with devotion. She imagined herself and other young girls waking one another before dawn, going together to worship Bhagavān, and seeking His grace. Her devotion was repeated, intimate, and steady.
To an outsider, it may have seemed like poetry or emotion. But inwardly, her mind was being trained to return again and again to one center — Bhagavān.
This is the spirit of abhyāsa-yoga. The mind may not yet be ready to see the entire universe as Bhagavān. But it can begin with one beloved form. Through repeated remembrance, singing, visualization, and love, the mind becomes steady.
Āṇḍāḷ’s devotion shows how iṣṭa-devatā upāsanā can absorb the heart and gradually transform life into worship.
5. Impact to Current Life
Many people today want a vast spiritual vision immediately. They may hear that Bhagavān is everywhere, that everything is divine, and that the whole universe is Viśvarūpa. But when they meet a difficult person, face inconvenience, or encounter something unpleasant, the mind cannot maintain that vision.
This is because rāga-dveṣa narrows the mind. We may say “everything is Bhagavān,” but internally we still divide life into what I like, what I hate, what I want, and what I reject.
This verse gives us a realistic starting point. If the mind cannot expand to the total, we can begin with one sacred focus. A chosen form of Bhagavān gives the mind a devotional center. Repeated practice slowly gathers the scattered mind.
In modern life, this is very relevant. Our attention is fragmented by phones, work, worries, opinions, and emotional reactions. Abhyāsa-yoga teaches that steadiness does not come by wishing. It comes by returning again and again to a chosen sacred focus.
6. Reflection
Is my mind broad enough to see the whole world as Bhagavān, or is it still caught in strong likes and dislikes?
Can I choose one sacred focus and return to it every day with sincerity?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose you want to learn music or cricket. You cannot become good just by trying once. You practice again and again.
In the same way, Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa says that if thinking of Bhagavān everywhere is difficult, we can start by thinking of one form of Bhagavān that we love — like Kṛṣṇa, Rāma, Śiva, Devī, or Gaṇeśa.
Every day, we remember that form, pray, and try again. That repeated practice is called abhyāsa.”
7B. Story
A child wanted to learn to draw a lotus.
The first day, the drawing did not look like a lotus.
The child felt sad and said, “I cannot do this.”
The teacher smiled and said, “Draw one lotus every day.”
So the child practiced daily. Some days the petals were uneven. Some days the stem was crooked. But slowly the hand became steady.
After many days, the child drew a beautiful lotus.
The teacher said, “See? Practice makes the hand steady.”
In the same way, when we remember Bhagavān again and again, the mind becomes steady.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What is one thing you became better at by practicing every day?
Can you remember Bhagavān for a few minutes every day?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: Alternative Step
Phrase: atha = on the other hand / if however
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now gives the next step for those who cannot practice the earlier discipline of steadily fixing the mind on Viśvarūpa īśvara.
Step 2: The Difficulty
Phrase: cittaṁ samādhātuṁ na śaknoṣi = you are not able to fix the mind
The difficulty is not lack of sincerity alone. The mind may still be narrow and restless because of rāga-dveṣa. It keeps returning to objects of attachment and aversion, so it cannot easily expand into Viśvarūpa contemplation.
Step 3: The Object of Meditation
Phrase: mayi sthiram = steadily upon Me
Here “Me” refers to Viśvarūpa īśvara. To fix the mind steadily on the total universe as Bhagavān requires a large and mature mind. If that is not yet possible, another practice is given.
Step 4: The Practice Given
Phrase: abhyāsayogena = through abhyāsa-yoga
Abhyāsa-yoga here means iṣṭa-devatā upāsanā — repeated meditation upon a chosen deity or sacred symbol. The mind is given one devotional focus to return to again and again.
Step 5: Forms of Abhyāsa
Phrase: abhyāsayogena = by the discipline of repeated practice
The focus may be a pratīmā, a personal form of Bhagavān with limbs like ours, or a pratīka, a symbol such as Sūrya or Śrīcakra. In either case, the aim is to train the mind inward through repeated contemplation.
Step 6: The Goal
Phrase: tato māmicchāptuṁ dhanañjaya = then seek to attain Me, O Dhanañjaya
Through this repeated practice, we seek to attain Bhagavān. This stage prepares the mind by giving it focus and inwardness. It is a lower rung than Viśvarūpa upāsanā, but it is a necessary and compassionate step for a mind that is not yet ready for the wider vision.

9E. Sandhi
चित्तम् + समाधातुम् = चित्तं समाधातुम्
Sandhi name: अनुस्वारादेशः; final म् is represented as अनुस्वारः before the following consonant स.
अभ्यासयोगेन + ततः = अभ्यासयोगेन ततः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; the words join without phonetic change.
माम् + इच्छ = मामिच्छ
Sandhi name: अनुस्वार/परसवर्णाभावः with direct joining; final म् joins before the vowel इ without visible change in Devanagari sandhi form.
इच्छ + आप्तुम् = इच्छाप्तुम्
Sandhi name: सवर्णदीर्घसन्धिः; final अ and initial आ combine into long आ.
माम् + इच्छाप्तुम् = मामिच्छाप्तुम्
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final म् joins with the following vowel इ without visible change.
9F. Samāsa
अभ्यासयोगेन = तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: अभ्यासरूपः योगः — the yoga in the form of repeated practice.
धनञ्जय = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: धनं जयति इति — one who conquers wealth; an epithet of Arjuna.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
समाधातुम् is a तुमुनन्त-कृदन्तम् from सम्-आ-धा, meaning “to fix” or “to place steadily.”
आप्तुम् is a तुमुनन्त-कृदन्तम् from आप्, meaning “to attain.”
शक्नोषि is लट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम् from शक् धातुः.
इच्छ is लोट्-लकारः, मध्यमपुरुषः, एकवचनम् from इष् धातुः, used as an instruction.
धनञ्जय is सम्बोधनप्रथमा, directly addressing Arjuna.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Dhruva’s Repeated Practice (Bhāgavata Purāṇa)
Dhruva was a young child when hurt and rejection pushed him toward the forest. His mother did not encourage anger. She directed him toward Bhagavān.
On the way, Dhruva met Nārada, who taught him how to meditate. Nārada gave him a mantra and instructed him in disciplined worship of Bhagavān Viṣṇu.
At first, Dhruva was only a child with pain in his heart. His mind could easily have wandered back to anger, family, or desire. But he practiced again and again. He sat, repeated the mantra, visualized Bhagavān, and returned the mind whenever it moved away.
His practice became deeper with time. What began as personal hurt slowly became one-pointed devotion.
Eventually, Bhagavān appeared before him.
Dhruva’s story shows the power of abhyāsa-yoga. The mind may not become steady in one day. But when it returns again and again to Bhagavān, it becomes capable of deep absorption.


2. Story of Kannappa Nāyaṉār
Kannappa was a hunter, simple and untrained in formal ritual. But his heart was filled with intense devotion to Bhagavān Śiva.
When he discovered a Śiva liṅga in the forest, he began worshipping in the only way he knew. He brought water in his mouth, offered forest food, and gave what he considered best.
To the world, his worship looked improper. But his mind was fully fixed on Bhagavān.
Day after day, he returned to that same sacred form. The liṅga became the center of his life.
When he saw blood flowing from the eye of the liṅga, he did not hesitate. In total devotion, he offered his own eye. When the other eye too seemed to bleed, he prepared to offer that as well.
This story shows the power of one-pointed devotion. Kannappa did not meditate on the entire universe as Bhagavān. His mind was absorbed in one form, and that form opened the door to total surrender.
3. Story of Pundalika and Viṭṭhala
Pundalika’s life is remembered in connection with Bhagavān Viṭṭhala of Paṇḍharpur.
At one stage, Pundalika was transformed by devotion and service. His mind became centered on dharma, service to his parents, and remembrance of Bhagavān.
When Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa came to bless him in the form of Viṭṭhala, Pundalika was serving his parents. Not wanting to interrupt that sacred duty, he placed a brick and asked Bhagavān to wait.
Bhagavān stood there, hands on hips, waiting with love.
The image of Viṭṭhala standing on the brick became a powerful focus for generations of devotees.
This is the beauty of iṣṭa-devatā upāsanā. One form becomes the center of remembrance. Through repeated singing, walking, pilgrimage, and worship, countless devotees turn their minds again and again toward Viṭṭhala.
4. Story of Sūrya Upāsanā and Sāmba
In Purāṇic tradition, Sāmba, the son of Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa, suffered from a severe disease and turned to Sūrya for healing and grace.
Sūrya is not merely a ball of fire in this form of worship. Sūrya becomes a sacred symbol, a visible support for meditation and devotion.
Through worship, discipline, and repeated turning toward Sūrya, Sāmba’s life was transformed.
This story shows the role of pratīka upāsanā. When the mind cannot hold the abstract or the total, it can hold a sacred symbol. The sun becomes a doorway through which the mind is gathered, purified, and lifted.
5. Story of a Devotee of Śrīcakra
In the Śrīvidyā tradition, many devotees worship Śrīcakra as a sacred symbol of Devī.
A devotee may sit before the yantra every day, not treating it as a mere geometric pattern, but as a sacred presence. The lines, triangles, and bindu become a support for meditation.
At first, the mind may wander. Thoughts of family, work, money, and worries may come repeatedly. But the devotee returns again and again to the center.
That returning is abhyāsa.
Over time, the symbol is no longer merely external. It becomes a doorway into inwardness. The extrovert mind learns to become quiet.
This illustrates what abhyāsa-yoga does: it gives the mind a sacred focus and trains it to return until steadiness becomes natural.
