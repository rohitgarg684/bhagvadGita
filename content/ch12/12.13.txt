1. Shloka
अद्वेष्टा सर्वभूतानां मैत्रः करुण एव च ।
निर्ममो निरहङ्कारः समदुःखसुखः क्षमी ॥12.13॥
adveṣṭā sarvabhūtānāṁ maitraḥ karuṇa eva ca ।
nirmamo nirahaṅkāraḥ samaduḥkhasukhaḥ kṣamī ॥12.13॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa begins describing the parā-bhakta as one who has no hatred toward any being, is friendly and compassionate, free from possessiveness and ego, balanced in pleasure and pain, and forgiving.
3. Meaning
From this verse onward, Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa describes the nature of the mature devotee — the parā-bhakta, the one who has gone through the spiritual disciplines and discovered the highest truth. This is no longer a description of an initial practice, but the character of one whose mind has been transformed.
The first quality is adveṣṭā sarvabhūtānām — one who has no hatred toward any being. This does not mean we should passively tolerate wrong actions. If correction is required, appropriate action may be taken. But the action should not be motivated by hatred, revenge, or the desire to hurt. Misbehavior may require correction, but the person does not require hatred. Even punishment, when unavoidable, should be guided by clarity and concern, not hatred.
The next qualities are maitraḥ and karuṇaḥ — friendly and compassionate. A mature devotee does not live with a hostile vision of the world. There is a basic goodwill toward all beings. When others suffer, compassion naturally arises. This friendliness and compassion are not selective; they flow from the understanding that all beings are included in the same total order of Bhagavān.
Then Bhagavān says nirmamaḥ and nirahaṅkāraḥ. Nirmamaḥ means free from possessiveness — the constant “mine-making” tendency. Nirahaṅkāraḥ means free from egoistic self-importance. When “I” and “mine” loosen, the mind becomes lighter and less defensive.
Finally, such a devotee is samaduḥkhasukhaḥ and kṣamī — balanced in sorrow and joy, and forgiving. Since the mind is not centered on ego and possessiveness, pleasure and pain do not throw it violently up and down. And because there is no hatred, forgiveness becomes natural.
4. Story
In the Mahābhārata, there is a powerful moment after the war that shows the spirit of adveṣṭā sarvabhūtānām.
After the terrible war of Kurukṣetra, the Pāṇḍavas had lost almost everything dear to them. Sons, relatives, teachers, friends, and warriors had fallen. The victory was heavy with grief.
Then came one more wound. Aśvatthāman, in anger and revenge, killed the sleeping sons of Draupadī. These children were innocent. Draupadī’s grief was unbearable.
Aśvatthāman was captured and brought before her. Everyone expected Draupadī to demand punishment. She had every reason to burn with hatred. Her children had been taken away unjustly.
But in that moment of great sorrow, Draupadī saw beyond her own pain.
She remembered that Aśvatthāman was the son of Droṇa. If he were killed, his mother Kṛpī would suffer the same agony that Draupadī herself was suffering. Draupadī did not approve of his action. She did not say that he had done no wrong. But she refused to let hatred become her response.
Her heart, even in grief, made room for another mother’s sorrow.
This is not weakness. It is greatness.
Draupadī’s response shows how one may condemn adharma without hatred toward the person. Pain was there. Injustice was there. Appropriate action was needed. But hatred did not rule her heart.
This beautifully reflects adveṣṭā, karuṇaḥ, and kṣamī.
5. Impact to Current Life
In modern life, hatred has become very easy to justify. We may hate people because of politics, religion, family conflict, workplace rivalry, social media arguments, or personal hurt. Sometimes we think hatred gives us strength, but it usually burns our own mind first.
This verse teaches a more mature response. We do not have to approve of wrong behavior. We do not have to become passive. We can correct, oppose, protect, punish, or set boundaries when needed. But hatred need not be the inner fuel.
This is especially important in parenting, leadership, teaching, and social life. A parent may correct a child. A manager may correct an employee. A society may punish a criminal. But if the action is driven by revenge, anger, and ego, it creates more damage. If action is guided by clarity, fairness, and concern for correction, it becomes dhārmic.
The verse also challenges our possessiveness and ego. Many emotional reactions come from “my people,” “my success,” “my insult,” “my opinion,” “my side.” When this grip loosens, the mind becomes more balanced and forgiving.
6. Reflection
Can I correct wrong behavior without hatred toward the person?
Where do “I” and “mine” make me defensive, possessive, or unforgiving?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose someone makes a mistake or behaves badly. We may have to tell them it is wrong. But we do not have to hate them.
Kṛṣṇa is saying that a good devotee does not keep hatred in the heart. Such a person is friendly, kind, and forgiving. That does not mean they allow wrong things. It means they correct with care, not with hatred.”
7B. Story
A child had a friend who broke a favorite pencil.
At first, the child became very angry and thought, “I will never talk to him again!”
Then the friend came and said, “I am sorry. I did not mean to break it.”
The child still felt sad, but slowly said, “You should be careful next time. But I forgive you.”
The friend felt relieved.
The pencil was broken, but the friendship was not broken.
Kṛṣṇa is teaching us to correct what is wrong, but not fill our heart with hatred.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
Have you ever forgiven someone who made a mistake?
Can you say something is wrong without hating the person?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: The Mature Devotee
Phrase: adveṣṭā = one who is free from hatred
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now begins describing the parā-bhakta. The first quality is absence of hatred. This does not mean absence of discrimination or absence of correction. It means hatred is not the inner attitude.
Step 2: Universal Scope
Phrase: sarvabhūtānām = toward all beings
No being should be kept in a private list of hatred. The scope is universal. We may respond differently to different behaviors, but hatred toward the being is not retained.
Step 3: Positive Goodwill
Phrase: maitraḥ = friendly
The absence of hatred is not cold indifference. It becomes a positive attitude of friendliness and goodwill. The mature devotee is not inwardly hostile to the world.
Step 4: Compassion
Phrase: karuṇa eva ca = and compassionate indeed
Compassion means sensitivity to the pain and struggle of others. Even when correction is required, the attitude is not retaliation but concern for what is right and healing.
Step 5: Freedom from Possessiveness and Ego
Phrase: nirmamo nirahaṅkāraḥ = free from “mine” and free from ego
Possessiveness and ego are major causes of hurt and hatred. When “mine” and “I am the doer / I am superior” are weakened, the mind becomes less reactive and more open.
Step 6: Balance and Forgiveness
Phrase: samaduḥkhasukhaḥ kṣamī = balanced in sorrow and joy, forgiving
Such a person is not violently tossed between pleasure and pain. Because the ego is light and hatred is absent, forgiveness becomes possible. The person can respond to life without being inwardly poisoned.

9E. Sandhi
करुणः + एव = करुण एव
Sandhi name: विसर्गलोपः / विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga is dropped before the following vowel ए.
निर्ममः + निरहङ्कारः = निर्ममो निरहङ्कारः
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final अः becomes ओ before the following soft consonant न.
निरहङ्कारः + समदुःखसुखः = निरहङ्कारः समदुःखसुखः
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; visarga remains before the following hard sibilant स्.
समदुःखसुखः + क्षमी = समदुःखसुखः क्षमी
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; visarga remains before the following hard consonant क्ष्.
9F. Samāsa
सर्वभूतानाम् = षष्ठी तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: सर्वेषां भूतानाम् — of all beings.
निरहङ्कारः = नञ् तत्पुरुष समासः
Breakdown: न अहङ्कारः यस्य / अहङ्काररहितः — one who is free from egoism.
समदुःखसुखः = द्वन्द्वगर्भित बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: दुःखं च सुखं च = दुःखसुखे; तयोः समः यः सः — one who is equal in sorrow and joy.
निर्ममः = नञ् तत्पुरुष / बहुव्रीहि sense
Breakdown: ममकाररहितः — one who is free from possessiveness.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
अद्वेष्टा is a तृच्-प्रत्ययान्त-कर्तृवाचक-नामपदम् from द्विष् धातुः, with negative prefix अ-, meaning “one who does not hate.”
क्षमी is formed with the इनि/इन् sense, meaning “one endowed with forgiveness” or “forbearing.”
All descriptive words in this verse are प्रथमा-एकवचनम्, qualifying the implied devotee.
भवति is understood elliptically in the anvaya.
10. More Stories
1. Story of Draupadī and Aśvatthāman (Mahābhārata)
After the war, Draupadī had already suffered beyond measure. She had seen humiliation, exile, war, and loss. Then came the unbearable news that her sons had been killed while asleep by Aśvatthāman.
Her grief was deep and raw.
When Aśvatthāman was captured and brought before her, the moment was charged with pain. Everyone knew what he had done. Everyone knew that Draupadī had the right to be angry.
But Draupadī looked beyond revenge.
She remembered Kṛpī, the mother of Aśvatthāman. If he were killed, another mother would weep as she was weeping. Draupadī did not excuse the crime, but she refused to let hatred become her response.
This is extraordinary compassion in the middle of suffering.
Her heart had room for justice, grief, and mercy at the same time. This is adveṣṭā, karuṇaḥ, and kṣamī in a living form.
2. Story of Vidura’s Compassionate Counsel (Mahābhārata)
Vidura lived in the Kuru court, surrounded by ambition, jealousy, and political blindness. He saw the growing adharma of Duryodhana and repeatedly warned Dhṛtarāṣṭra.
His words were often sharp, but never hateful.
He did not flatter wrongdoing. He did not remain silent out of fear. Again and again he spoke what was right, trying to protect the kingdom from destruction.
Yet he was not motivated by anger or revenge. His counsel came from concern — for the Pāṇḍavas, for the Kauravas, for Dhṛtarāṣṭra, and for dharma.
This is the subtlety of maitraḥ and karuṇaḥ. Friendliness does not always mean pleasant words. Compassion sometimes speaks firmly. But the inner motive is welfare, not hatred.
3. Story of Bhagavān Buddha and Aṅgulimāla
Aṅgulimāla was feared by everyone. He had become a violent robber, and people trembled at his name.
One day, Bhagavān Buddha walked calmly into the forest where Aṅgulimāla lived. The robber ran toward him, but strangely, he could not catch up, though Buddha appeared to be walking slowly.
Aṅgulimāla shouted, “Stop!”
Buddha replied, “I have stopped. You stop.”
Those words pierced him.
Buddha did not look at him with hatred. He saw the suffering and ignorance behind the violence. Through compassion and wisdom, he transformed Aṅgulimāla’s life.
Aṅgulimāla became a monk.
This story shows that hatred does not transform. Compassion guided by wisdom can transform even a violent person.
4. Story of Mahāvīra’s Forgiveness
Bhagavān Mahāvīra is remembered for extraordinary ahiṁsā and forgiveness. Many times he faced insult, hardship, and physical suffering, but he did not respond with hatred.
His discipline was not weakness. It was mastery over reaction.
When people misunderstood him or caused pain, he did not allow resentment to take root. His vision was that beings act under ignorance, fear, and bondage. Therefore, the response should not be hatred, but compassion and firmness in non-injury.
This reflects kṣamī — forgiveness and forbearance.
Forgiveness here does not mean denying wrong. It means refusing to poison oneself with hatred.
5. Story of Sant Eknāth and the Spitting Man
Sant Eknāth was once walking after taking a sacred bath. A man, wanting to provoke him, spat on him.
Eknāth did not become angry. He calmly went back, bathed again, and returned.
The man spat again.
This happened repeatedly. Each time, Eknāth remained calm and went back to bathe.
Finally, the man broke down. He could not understand such patience. He asked forgiveness.
Eknāth did not carry hatred toward him. His patience exposed the other person’s anger and transformed it.
This story shows samaduḥkhasukhaḥ and kṣamī. The insult did not make him lose balance. His forgiveness became a teaching.
