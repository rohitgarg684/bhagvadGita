1. Shloka
ये तु धर्म्यामृतमिदं यथोक्तं पर्युपासते ।
श्रद्दधाना मत्परमा भक्तास्तेऽतीव मे प्रियाः ॥12.20॥
ye tu dharmyāmṛtamidaṁ yathoktaṁ paryupāsate ।
śraddadhānā matparamā bhaktāste'tīva me priyāḥ ॥12.20॥
2. One-Line Meaning
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa concludes that those devotees who, with śraddhā and with Bhagavān as the supreme goal, sincerely follow this immortal dharma as taught are exceedingly dear to Him.
3. Meaning
This verse is the concluding verse of the twelfth chapter. Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa has already taught the full range of bhakti-yoga: karma-yoga, upāsanā-yoga, and jñāna-yoga. He has also described the qualities of the parā-bhakta, the mature devotee who has gone through these disciplines and gained inner freedom.
Now He gathers the whole teaching and calls it dharmyāmṛtam — the immortal dharma. It is dharmya because it is a righteous, valid, scriptural way of life. It is amṛtam because it leads to immortality, meaning freedom from saṁsāra. This is not merely a list of good manners; it is the way of life that culminates in mokṣa.
The phrase idaṁ yathoktam means “this, as taught.” Bhagavān is referring to the teaching already unfolded: the stages of bhakti-yoga and the qualities of the parā-bhakta. These qualities are not independent moral decorations. They are the natural expression of a mind that has gone through karma-yoga, upāsanā, and jñāna.
Those who follow this teaching are śraddadhānāḥ — endowed with śraddhā. Śraddhā is not blind belief. It is trust in the śāstra and the guru long enough to live the teaching and discover its truth. They are also matparamāḥ — those for whom Bhagavān is the supreme goal. Bhagavān is not one interest among many; Bhagavān is the final end of life.
Such devotees are atīva me priyāḥ — exceedingly dear to Bhagavān. This is the final statement of the chapter: the dearest devotee is the one who receives the teaching with śraddhā, lives it, matures through it, and comes to abide in Bhagavān as the highest truth.
4. Story
In the Rāmāyaṇa, the life of Bharata beautifully reflects the spirit of living a teaching as dharma, with Bhagavān as the supreme goal.
When Bharata returned to Ayodhyā and discovered what had happened, he was shaken. Rāma had been sent to the forest, Daśaratha had died in grief, and the kingdom had come to Bharata because of Kaikeyī’s demand.
Bharata could have accepted the throne. The ministers, citizens, and royal machinery were all available. He had the outer opportunity to rule.
But his heart did not move toward power.
He went to the forest and begged Rāma to return. For Bharata, Rāma was not merely an elder brother; Rāma was dharma itself. If Rāma would not return because of His commitment to Daśaratha’s word, Bharata would not violate that dharma either.
So he took Rāma’s pādukās, placed them on the throne, and ruled only as a caretaker.
This was not symbolic for one day. For fourteen years, Bharata lived with restraint. He did not enjoy the kingdom as personal possession. He waited for Rāma. Every act of administration was done in Rāma’s name. His life was governed by śraddhā, dharma, and commitment to Bhagavān Rāma as the supreme center.
This is dharmyāmṛtam paryupāsate in living form. Bharata did not merely admire dharma; he lived it. He did not merely love Rāma emotionally; he made Rāma the highest reference point for life.
Such devotion is not casual affection. It is a life shaped by Bhagavān.
5. Impact to Current Life
Many people appreciate spiritual teachings, but appreciation alone does not transform life. We may like the Gītā, quote verses, admire saints, and enjoy spiritual ideas. But the question is: do we live the teaching?
This verse asks us to move from admiration to assimilation. Yathoktam paryupāsate means we take the teaching as it has been given and stay with it. We do not merely pick what is emotionally pleasing and ignore what challenges our ego.
In modern life, śraddhā is difficult because we are trained to be impatient. We want quick results. We want to test everything immediately by personal comfort. But deep transformation requires trust, time, and steady practice.
The phrase matparamāḥ is also very important. We may say spirituality matters, but often career, money, image, comfort, family approval, or personal success become the real supreme goals. This verse invites us to gently examine what actually governs our choices.
When Bhagavān becomes the highest goal, life becomes reorganized. Work remains, family remains, responsibilities remain, but they are no longer disconnected from spiritual growth. Everything becomes part of the journey toward freedom.
6. Reflection
Do I merely admire the teaching, or am I trying to live it as a dharma?
What is truly parama for me — Bhagavān and mokṣa, or some temporary worldly goal?
7. How to Teach a Child
7A. Explanation Script
“Suppose your teacher gives you good advice: be kind, tell the truth, do your work carefully, and do not hurt others.
It is not enough to say, ‘That is good advice.’ We have to try to live it.
Kṛṣṇa is saying that the people who trust His teaching and try to live it with love are very dear to Him. They do not just listen; they practice.”
7B. Story
A child’s teacher said, “Every day, do one kind thing without asking for praise.”
The child liked the idea.
The next day, the child helped a classmate pick up fallen books. No one saw it.
At first, the child thought, “No one praised me.”
Then the child remembered, “Teacher said to do kindness because it is right, not because someone claps.”
So the child smiled quietly.
Day after day, the child tried to follow the teaching. Sometimes it was easy. Sometimes the child forgot. But the child kept trying.
Slowly, kindness became natural.
That is what Kṛṣṇa is teaching. A good devotee listens with trust and then lives the teaching.
7C. Kid-friendly Reflection
What is one good teaching you can practice today?
Can you do something right even if no one praises you?
8. Detailed Meaning
Step 1: The Devotees Being Addressed
Phrase: ye tu = but those who
Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa now speaks of those devotees who receive and live the teaching that has been unfolded in this chapter. This is the concluding statement.
Step 2: The Teaching as Immortal Dharma
Phrase: dharmyāmṛtam idaṁ = this immortal dharma
The teaching is dharmya because it is righteous, scriptural, and aligned with spiritual growth. It is amṛtam because it leads to immortality — freedom from saṁsāra. The qualities described are not ordinary social virtues alone; they belong to the path that culminates in mokṣa.
Step 3: As Taught
Phrase: yathoktam = as taught
The teaching is to be understood as it has been unfolded. It includes the full bhakti-yoga ladder and the characteristics of the parā-bhakta. We should not distort the teaching according to convenience.
Step 4: Living the Teaching
Phrase: paryupāsate = they worship / follow / live with reverence
Here the sense is not merely ritual worship. It means reverentially embracing and practicing this teaching. The devotee lives in alignment with this dharma.
Step 5: Śraddhā
Phrase: śraddadhānāḥ = endowed with śraddhā
Śraddhā is trust in the teaching and the teacher. It allows us to stay with the discipline even before the full result is visible. Without śraddhā, the mind will keep doubting, dropping, and changing direction.
Step 6: Bhagavān as Supreme Goal
Phrase: matparamāḥ = having Me as the supreme goal
For these devotees, Bhagavān is the highest end. Other goals may exist at the transactional level, but they are not ultimate. The life-direction is toward Bhagavān and mokṣa.
Step 7: Exceedingly Dear
Phrase: bhaktāste'tīva me priyāḥ = those devotees are exceedingly dear to Me
Bhagavān concludes with the highest praise. Those who live this immortal dharma with śraddhā and with Bhagavān as the supreme goal are not merely dear; they are exceedingly dear.

9E. Sandhi
धर्म्य + अमृतम् = धर्म्यामृतम्
Sandhi name: सवर्णदीर्घसन्धिः; final अ and initial अ combine into आ.
धर्म्यामृतम् + इदम् = धर्म्यामृतमिदम्
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; final म् joins directly with the following vowel इ without visible change.
यथा + उक्तम् = यथोक्तम्
Sandhi name: गुणसन्धिः; final आ with initial उ gives ओ in the combined form.
भक्ताः + ते = भक्तास्ते
Sandhi name: विसर्गसन्धिः; final visarga becomes स् before the dental consonant त्.
ते + अतीव = तेऽतीव
Sandhi name: पूर्वरूपसन्धिः; final ए remains and the following अ is elided, marked by अवग्रहः.
अतीव + मे = अतीव मे
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; no special phonetic change.
मे + प्रियाः = मे प्रियाः
Sandhi name: संहितायाम् पदसन्धिः; no special phonetic change.
9F. Samāsa
धर्म्यामृतम् = कर्मधारय / तत्पुरुष sense
Breakdown: धर्म्यम् अमृतम् — the righteous teaching that is immortal / the dharma that leads to immortality.
यथोक्तम् = अव्ययीभाव समासः
Breakdown: यथा उक्तम् — as stated / as taught.
मत्परमाः = बहुव्रीहि समासः
Breakdown: अहं परमः येषां ते — those for whom I am the supreme goal.
9G. Other Grammatical Aspects
पर्युपासते is लट्-लकारः, प्रथमपुरुषः, बहुवचनम्, आत्मनेपदम् from परि-उप-आस् धातुः.
श्रद्दधानाः is a शानच्-प्रत्ययान्त form, meaning “having śraddhā” or “endowed with trust.”
उक्तम् in यथोक्तम् is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त-कृदन्तम् from वच् धातुः.
भक्ताः is a क्तप्रत्ययान्त form from भज् धातुः.
अतीव is an अव्ययम् intensifying प्रियाः, meaning “exceedingly dear.”
10. More Stories
1. Story of Bharata and the Pādukās
When Bharata returned to Ayodhyā, he did not find the joy of a coronation. Instead, he found grief, confusion, and moral shock.
Rāma had gone to the forest. Daśaratha was gone. The throne stood empty, and the kingdom was waiting.
Everyone looked to Bharata.
He could have said, “This has come to me by destiny.” He could have sat on the throne and ruled as king. Many would have accepted it.
But Bharata’s heart would not allow it.
He went to the forest and fell at Rāma’s feet, begging Him to return. Rāma refused, because Daśaratha’s word had to be protected.
Then Bharata did something extraordinary. He took Rāma’s pādukās back to Ayodhyā, placed them on the throne, and ruled as servant, not owner.
For fourteen years, he lived with one reference point: Rāma. His life was not guided by ambition, comfort, or personal identity.
This story shows śraddadhānaḥ and matparamaḥ. Bharata trusted dharma and made Bhagavān Rāma the supreme goal of his life.
2. Story of Śabala’s Devotion to Vasiṣṭha
In the Rāmāyaṇa tradition, Viśvāmitra once visited the āśrama of Vasiṣṭha. There he saw the divine cow Śabala, who could provide all that was needed for hospitality.
Viśvāmitra desired Śabala and asked Vasiṣṭha to give her to him. When Vasiṣṭha refused, the king tried to take her by force.
Śabala was pulled away, but her heart remained with Vasiṣṭha.
She cried out to her master. She did not think, “The king is powerful, so I should follow power.” Her devotion and loyalty were not based on fear or convenience.
Vasiṣṭha told her that if she wished, she could resist. From her arose great strength, and Viśvāmitra’s force was overcome.
This story shows the power of fidelity to dharma and guru. Śabala’s loyalty was not sentimental weakness. It was unwavering alignment with the rightful center.
In 12.20, the devotee is matparamaḥ — one for whom Bhagavān is supreme. Śabala’s story gives us a simple image: when the heart knows its true refuge, it does not run after power.
3. Story of Upamanyu and the Guru’s Instruction
Upamanyu was a young student under the guidance of his guru. His teacher tested him repeatedly.
When food was restricted, Upamanyu tried different ways to sustain himself. Each time, the guru corrected him. Finally, through hardship and mistake, Upamanyu suffered and fell into a well.
In that helpless condition, he did not abandon trust in the guru or the sacred path. He prayed, and by divine grace he was restored.
When he returned, the guru was pleased with his endurance, faith, and obedience.
The story is not about blind suffering. It is about śraddhā — the willingness to stay with a discipline until its deeper purpose is understood.
In spiritual life, we often want teachings that are immediately pleasant. But dharmyāmṛtam must be followed with patience. Upamanyu’s story shows how śraddhā carries a seeker through difficulty.
4. Story of Sāvitrī Following Dharma
Sāvitrī chose Satyavān as her husband, even though she was warned that his life would be short.
She did not choose based on convenience. Once she had recognized dharma, she stood by it with clarity.
When the destined day came, Satyavān fell in the forest. Yama came to take his life. Sāvitrī followed Yama with steadiness, humility, intelligence, and devotion.
Yama asked her to turn back, but she continued, speaking words of dharma with great composure.
She did not argue wildly. She did not collapse in grief. She did not abandon her commitment.
Moved by her wisdom and fidelity, Yama granted boons, and finally Satyavān’s life was restored.
Sāvitrī’s story shows what it means to live dharma with śraddhā. She did not merely talk about dharma; she followed it through fear, grief, and uncertainty.
5. Story of Pothana Offering the Bhāgavatam to Rāma
Pothana was a poet and devotee who composed the Telugu Bhāgavatam. Such a work could have brought fame, patronage, and royal approval.
A ruler desired that the work be dedicated to him.
But Pothana’s heart was clear. The Bhāgavatam was not written for worldly praise. It belonged to Bhagavān.
He offered it to Bhagavān Rāma.
This was not stubbornness for personal pride. It was matparamatvam. Bhagavān was the supreme recipient, not a worldly patron.
Through this act, Pothana showed that even literary talent, learning, and artistic beauty become sacred when offered to Bhagavān.
This story beautifully reflects 12.20. The devotee with śraddhā follows the immortal dharma and keeps Bhagavān as the highest goal.
